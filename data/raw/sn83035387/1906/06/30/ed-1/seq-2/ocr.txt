AS TO PROF. COUNCIL.
The shocking and brutal account
that is given of the cruel treatment
received by Rev. Reverdy C. Ransom,
pastor of the Charles Street A. M. E.
church, Boston, Mass., while travel
ing through Tennessee places it al
most beyond anything of recent oc
curence in the south. We know of
insults and abuse imposed upon in
offensive Afro-Americans in time of
travel, but here is a citizen beaten
and bruised by white passengers for
occupying a seat in a Pullman car.
Purchasing a ticket in Boston includ
ing Pullman service he quietly starts
on a journey to the south where he
was to deliver a lecture at Normal,
Ala. But. before reaching this place,
he is mobbed and driven from his
seat only because known as an Afro-
American. It seems hardly possible
to find words to account for the de
liberate coolness of this barbarity re
vealed in its criminal audacity. One
brute preys upon another, because of
its native tendency, but when the
human in man, trained and developed
under the refining and enlightened
influences of this professed Christian
age, vents its spleen upon that which
is heir and joint heir with Christ, it
leaves us to question the integrity of
the American protestant religion.
Americans have protested against the
false and spurious in the Christian
church and have contended for a pure
and undefiled worship. They have
protested against vain hypocracy and
have declared avowed allegiance to
the faith as contained in the_ golden
rule and taught by the emaculate
Saviour, but as though God Almighty
were void of those supreme attributes
which constitute deity, they spurn his
mandates and hold him in open de
fiance. But God will not be mocked
and southern white men can find no
eicsse in reason nor justifiable
ground for the wicked and inhuman
practices which they daily perpetrate
against our people of the south. Rev.
Reverdy C. Ransom is a part of the
people, and is heir to every right and
privilege that is common to man and
American citizenship. What folly
then, what blindness, that a dominant
class should wreak vengeance upon
the innocent and helpless and call
themselves a Christian people. What
depravity of soul meanness of
heart to outrage humanity because of
racial difference. Persecuted for
righteousness sake, Dr. Ransom suf
fers from injuries inflicted upon his
person by bad southern whit£ men
and he suffers from that same brutal
ity of race hatred which ere long must
call for retribution. The southern
white people boast of their Saxon
blood, but dare we call them brave in
the cowardly act of assaulting a man
of the wear.er race? All that we pro
fess in honor of what we are is mere
hollow mockery and the south itself
being a parr of the nation, the nation
has but to hang its head in humilia
tion and shame (hat some of its best
citizens, though of another race, are
made victims to the meanest treat
ment known to the constitution of our
great government. Very sad. too, is
the state of affairs, when men es our
own race like the principal of the
Normal industrial school will so
abuse themselves as to compromise
their manhood and sense of honor in
order to continue themselves in favor
with the oppressor. Professor Conn
cil should have in mercy and kindness
cared for Dr. Ransom regardless of his
condition, drunk or sober, lie should
have braved the wrongs imposed upon
the unfortunate minister, but instead
of which, he quailed. Prof. Council
is to be pitied.
THE PRESIDENT’S ASSAILANTS.
President Roosevelt just*now is
being assailed by a class who today
are less informed in regard to na
tional affairs than our party leaders.
It is quite out of place too that there
are those who show themselves more
active and hasty in criticism and de
nunciations than those who are im
mediately concerned. That the col
lege professor and president should
put themselves foremost to submit
charges and to denounce the nation’s
head, proves a degree of heartless im
modesty. Just why our literary men
should seek to be foremost in this re
spect, becomes a matter of serious
inquiry. The press of the country has
been virulent and merciless at times;
it has made its thrusts with an un
pardonable viciousness, while the
public has regarded it as a sort of
newspaper strategy- or political ex
pediency. But the professional man
finds other motives. He certainly is
not in a position to comprehend the
breadth and scope of great state
questions better than ethers. Yet he
ventures to offer comments and criti
cisms, he ventures to question the
wisdom of great measures and im
pose such strictures as may be in
keeping with his judgment. In this
he finds his opportunity to win dis
tinction and joins hands with those
who plot mischief through malignity
and envy. With an eagerness to gain
notoriety and be called “smart,” he
becomes the assailant of the nation s
head and thus be termed our modern
Solon. But the part thus played is
empty- of all honest purpose and be
trays a vanity and arrogance verging
on maliciousness. Our ablest, best
minds are disposed to be generous to
men in exalted stations and would
rather concede the right of opinion
and jurisdiction than become his tra
ducer. Great is President Roosevelt —
great in purpose and action because’
he reveal^ in the tenor of his w-ay a
disposition to deal righteously, to
maintain the courage of conviction, to
shield the wronged in the protection
of the right, and in the administration
of his official duties we believe he
would^rather be right than be presi
dent of these United States. Let
those who stand ever waiting to send
the dagger to the heart, pause but a
moment lest the sins of their own in
iquity be visited upon them.
