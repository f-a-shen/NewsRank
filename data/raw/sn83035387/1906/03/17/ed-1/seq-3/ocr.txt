Ladies Doing Splendid Work.
Editor Gazette, Dear Sir.—The
Ladies' Benevolent club has been or
ganized less than three months and
yet our success has been more than
we expected. Financially, we are do
ing well and each meeting brings us
an increase in membership. This
alone is encouraging to us, for it tells
that our work meets the approval of
the public in general. We are work
ing earnestly and have an aim in
view: helping the needy. I think a
word of appreciation and encourage
ment should be said of the ladies who
compose this club. I have had the
extreme pleasure of working with
them for two years and have always
found them to be unselfish and self
sacrificing in their dispositions, never
asking others to give until they have
given themselves; never asking others
to contribute more liberally than they
contribute themselves. God has
blessed our every effort, and the
smiles and expressions of thankfulness
and appreciation coming from the
lips of the needy and distressed more
than repay us for our effort.
MRS. J. M. GILMERE.

Joe Gans and Congressman Hardwick,
While traveling in a Pullman car
not long ago Congressman Hardwick,
of Georgia, the smallest man in the
house of representatives at Washing
ton, found himself a fellow passenger
with a well dressed Afro-American.
This was not agreeable to the Georg
ian, who was further “riled” on seeing
him in the dining car. Both returned
to the Pullman about the same time
and then Mr. Hardwick went to the
conductor and asked that the Afro-
American be put out of the car. “We
can’t do that, sir,” the conductor an
swered. “Well, if he gets near me I’m
going to wipe up the car with him,”
declared the Georgian. “I won’t have
him around me. Who is he?” “That’s
Joe Gans, champion lightweight
pugilist,” answered the conductor, and
Mr. Hardwick concluded not to “wipe
up the car” with the quiet looking fel
low passenger whose deportment was
so much better than his own.
