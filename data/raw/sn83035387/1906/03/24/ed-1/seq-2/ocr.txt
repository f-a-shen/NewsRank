NOSE AND EYES.
There is said to be a right and a
wrong way for every thing we do;
hence the sooner we get down to right
doing the better for all concerned.
Humanity is heir to ills innumerable
which with care and precaution may
be eliminated. We need not endure
ills and' infirmities when we learn to
avoid the effects inducing them. An
eminent medical professor in Eng
land has recently given strong rea
sons why nose-blowing should be
made a matter of special care. First
one nostril and then the other should
be blown, one after the other but not
both at the same time. The two nasal
passages are not to be closed at the
same time. If obstructed as in the
case of cold, the back of the throat at
once becomes filled with compressed
air and this together with the dis
charge and the microbes which it con
tains is more than likely to be driven
through the eustachian tube into the
middle ear and lead to serious results.
In many cases ear trouble, and even
Intense headache, are brought on from
the straining of the eustachian tube,
for it is from this tube mainly that
disease of the ear is contracted. It
is also a scientific and hygienic truth
that the eyes should receive equal
care. From the very structure of the
eye, so necessary for comfort, Mother
Nature has afforded it means and pro
tection which must not be abused.
The health and care of the eyes de
pend altogether upon the way we
treat them. The eye suiters very ma
terially from mistreatment as from
imprudent use and abuse. The sensi
tive retina is the screen, and the optic
perve carries the impression of this
picture to the visual center to the
back part of the brain. Any severe
pressure upon the pupil of the eye is
hurtful. To relieve the eye from pain
it is never right to rub across the
cornea, but to draw the finger direct-
IV from it. The experiment will af
ford a gradual relief not otherwise
felt. In that way the retina is re
lieved from irritation and possible
harm. The eye and nose are largely
in sympathy with each other, for they
alike lead to the nerve of the brain
। y means of which w*e see and smell.
Treat these well and with greater
•are and we preserve unto ourselves
more surely our five senses whose
service jointly add to our physical and
mental happiness. In blowing our nose
let us blow one nostril and then the
other, and in relieving the eye of pain
or irritation, hands off the globe and
draw them away from either side.
FIND A HOME ON THE FARM.
It should be largely the choice of all
Afro-Americans today who desire per
manence in business and to enjoy an
independent life, that they seek to find
homes on the farm. There is no life
so free from incumbrance and none
that affords so much freedom as that
of the farmer. There are thousands
of Afro-Americans in the city who are
toiling day in and day out as hirelings
depending upon some boss to furnish
them employment. There are thous
ands who serve as porters in business
houses, in banks and stores whose
continuance In these places depend
solely upon their ability to please.
Many again depend upon doing chores
and catch-work here and there for a
livelihood, living from hand to mouth,
scarcely realizing means enough to
furnish bread for the family. Exposed
to countless allurements and expenses
unlooked for, they find life hardly
worth the living. Others find em
ployment only six or nine months in
the year, with little hope of covering
their indebtedness until perhaps the
courts are called to have a hand in
matters which lead on to serious com
plications. The city police, too, are
ever on the alert to arrest vagrants
or to bring to account persons of
seemingly questionable character,
which leave many a man in the light
of a mere pauper or a dependent. Few
men prefer to so live. But in this
broad and open country where living
is quite possible, where thrift and
plenty may be assured and where
freedom and independence may be en
joyed in the home, we ask is it net
better by far that the above class at;
Afro-Americans choose living on the
farm and there remain, unless he
sees in the change something better
and more advantageous? We live to
enjoy life, we live as helpers in this
world and for whatever possible good
we may accomplish; because of our
being here. Then does it not stand
us in hand to so situate ourselves that
we shall command for our families
those blessings which all human
hearts must naturally crave? No man
can be much of a helper to his race
when he himself is a dependent. He
must therefore spread himself out, he
must be his own boss, seek an inde
pendent life and hustle to increase
his means and become a helper with
others. In this respect he accom
plishes the greatest good for the
greatest number. How shall he best
succeed in such a venture? We an
swer by adapting himself to those con
ditions most suited to his circum
stances in life. Some of the grand
est and wealthiest men of the world
have come from the farm. Not con
tent with tilling the soil and raising
a crop, the prudent farmer buys the
land and begins at once to replenish
his barn and to furnish himself such
supplies as shall fortify himself
against want and dependence. Year
after year he gathers fresh recruits
from the field, until he is known as
one of the leading producers of the
country. With a full supply of cattle,
hogs, sheep and poultry, he feeds our
home and foreign markets and offers
an example quite worthy of emula
tion by the struggling laborer who
toils as a servant day after day. It
is the industrious farmer whose cof
fers are never empty; it is he who
goes and comes at his own beck and
call and finds contentment at home.
Man in his earliest stage knew no
other occupation save that of the
farmer and it was he who as the early
settler of America, built up our coun
try and made it a bulwark against the
attendant embarrassments which so
frequently beset the pioneers of
many countries. The farmer has giv
en an incomparable example for man
kind in every cline and country and he
who will diligently apply himself to
' duty's call need not suffer. The
! wealth of this nation eminated from
| the hand of the sturdy old farmer
, whose faith never flagged in the hope
j which he felt in the growing prospect
I which ever beckoned him on to a life
'of gain and independence. Afro
। Americans need to realize more sure
! ly that from terra firma comes to them
ino uncertain wealth. Their farms dot
the lands of Mississippi, Louisiana,
' Florida, Georgia, the Carolinas, Ala
: bama, Arkansas, Tennessee and Ken-
I tucky, and it is the Afro-American
farmers of the south today more than
any other people who are stimulating
trade and commerce with the quicken
ing forces of industry and enterprise.
Thousands of ex-slaves own rich fields
of cotton, wheat and corn together
with hundreds of heads of cattle and
hogs and afford palpable proof that
of all occupations for the black man
in America seeking a life of ease and
independence, that of the farmer
commends itself first.

Coffeyville has refused to obey the
order of the supreme court, rendered

lic schools, and our people are filing
protests with the clerk of the court.

separated in the schools of the first
class cities. Coffeyville is a second
class city. The children of Bud.
Cartwright were refused admission to
the school. The court ordered them
admitted.

IN MEMORY OF PAUL LAURENCE
DUNBAR.
Oh Paul! how thy death doth grieve
me,
Thy place we can never fill;
But God, in His wisdom has called
thee,
We humbly submit to His will.
May thy soul rest in peace forever.
Thou who wert beloved by all;
Forget thee, Oh Paul! we will never.
Until the last soul hears its call.
We mourn, for our hearts are broken.
Our eyes are filled with tears;
Thy name with love shall be spoken
Through ail the eternal years.
Called in the midst of his labor
He quietly laid down his pen:
And offered a prayer to our Saviour,
His life's work was at an end.
My work 1 will leave for another.
The Angels are beckoning to me:
Farewell! my loved ones, my mother;
Oh God! I will soon be with Thee.
While his mother’s heart was aching,
In the stillness of the night;
And just as the day seemed breaking.
His spirit took its flight.'
Oh God! hear our prayers from afar,
We ask in Thy goodness and grace:
Send another Paul Laurence Dunbar,
To sing the sweet songs of our race.
—Charles S. Smith.

NO CONTROL
Over Insurance or Other
State Corporations
BY GOVERNMENT.
This Is the Conclusion Reached by
the Judiciary Committee of
the House.
Washington, D. C. —That there is
no constitutional authority for fed
eral control of insurance or other state
corporations other than railroads, is
to be the conclusion reported to the
house by the judiciary committee.
The report has been drafted by Chair
man Jenkins, of the committee, and
is now in the hands of members of the
committee for their perusal. An un
official poll of the members of the
committee indicates that with practic
ally no exceptions they concur in the
correctness of this conclusion. The
report of Mr. Jenkins may therefore
be taken as the basis of the answer
the committee is to make to the house
in compliance with the instructions
of that body.
The report collates all of the im
portant court decisions on the ques
tions involved, treats each exhaus
tively and reduces the whole prob
lem to these two principles. “The su
preme court of the United States has
declared, and has never been
shaken or weakened in maintaining,
first, that insurance is not commerce,
and, second, that congress cannot im
pair the police powers of the states.”
The advocates of the federal regu
lation concede, according to the re
port, that insurance is not commerce.
“The supreme court,” the report says,
“has had great difficulty in marking
the boundary line in each case as it
has arisen between the power of the
state and the commerce clause of the
federal constitution.
“In each case, jealously protecting
each power as they came in conflict
and not until now, in a moment of ex
citement and intense interest, has it
ever been suggested that congress can
impair the police power of the states.”
The report sets forth section 8 of
article 1 of the constitution as confer
ring the only power possessed by con
gress to regulate commerce. “The
question as to whether or not insur-
ance is commerce has passed beyond
the realm of argument, because the
supreme court of the United States
has said many times for a great num
ber of years that insurance is not
commerce.”
