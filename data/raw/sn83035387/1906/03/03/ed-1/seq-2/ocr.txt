The Springfield riot of the past
■week is but the natural outcome of the
one two years ago promoted by the
police of that city and abetted by the
local members of the state militia.
This same was true of this week’s mob
demonstration there. There would
have beeh one or more lynchings this
time but for the restraining influence
of our Ohio anti-lynching law. That
(Clarke) county’s experience with the
law as the result of the Dixon lynch
ing two year’s ago, “educated” it along
a certain obvious line, and has had a
salutary effect upon its officials par
ticularly, and upon the vast majority
of its tax payers. The “riff-raff” of
the town composed the mob which
the sentiment and influence of the
community made the officers outwit
and safely landed their prisoners in
the Dayton jail.
Mrs. Tom L. Johnson, the wife of
our mayor, is a splendid speciman of
the best American womanhood, charit
able to an extraordinary degree as her
many good deeds in this community
to the very poor and deserving with
out reference to race, class or re
ligion, attest. Mrs. Johnson is the
fortunate possessor of a good heart
and excellent disposition that prompt
her many kind deeds and repay her a
thousand fold with the innate satis
faction that means so much to the
true Christian lady and gentleman.
The editor of The Gazette was more
than ever before impressed with these
facts when in attendance upon the
funeral of little James Tyler, noted
in our last issue. It spoke volumes
for both the mayor, his splendid wife
and family, which we would not here
chronicle if we could because we know
it would not prove pleasing to per
sons of such culture and refinement.
Our only excuse for the publication of
this brief reference, is that we too, in
common with the dead lad's parents,
Mr. and Mrs. James Tyler, and oth
ers want Mrs. Johnson particularly,
to know that her many kindnesses and
consideration during the boy's illness
and while his little body laid in the
beautiful Johnson home, and after
wards, is thoroughly appreciated.
May she live long and continue to en
joy good health is our earnest wish.
TILLMAN’S BRUTAL ATTACK.
Seldom in the history of legislative
affairs has ,it been recorded that a
United States senator is called to vote
down his own resolution. But Senator
Tillman during the present session
has offered himself as the shameful
and humiliating spectacle, and that,
too, before the gaze of learned sena
tors. Mr. Tillman was off (not
drunk), sadly off, and now his discon
certed friends are offering apologies
that the man's mind was overtaxed
and he scarcely knew who was who.
Passionate and brutal in his methods,
he makes friends for the very cause
which he opposes. It was so the other
day when he again assailed the presi
dent. So bitter and rash was the man
that the whole senate became as
tounded and shamefully disgusted.
Against such methods the entire body
stands always opposed. But Senator
Tillman could never be made to re
alize this fact. The public is well
aware that Tillman indulges to ex
treme his personal antipathies, and
being at personal enmity to the presi
dent, because on one occasion Mr.
Roosevelt caused a White House in
vitation to be withdrawn, Tillman has
neglected no occasion to exhibit a
mad spirit of resentment. So he
scored the president on account of the
recent ejection of Mrs. Minor Morris
from the White House and for writing
a letter to Fitzsimmons, the prize
fighter. Tillman’s scathing stood
without a parallel in the senate. He
raved like a maniac and caused sena
tors to quail, suspecting that the
South Carolinan had hopelessly lost
his eqvflibrum. Honorable senators
entreated him to desist in his ravings
and Senator Hale and Senator Daniel
remonstrated with him against such
abuse, but the man refused to be
quiet. He, however, lost balance and
finally concluded his terrible phillippic
by a sudden exit from the senate. Dur
ing his absence honorable members
seemed appalled and sat in silence as
if overcome by the shock of an earth
quake. Tillman knew now for once
that he had overreached himself and
was slow and hesitating in a speedy
return. So unexpectedly did he leave
the floor that gentlemen felt some
seriousness that heart failure was the
cause. Returning the next day to the
griff r** be introduced a resolution
calling for an investigation of the
Morris affair. His near friends re
proved him for bls conduct on the pre-
vious day and he consequently apolo
giled for his anger. The man, how
ever, had not yet discovered how
shamefully he had encroached upon
the privileges of the senate until Sen
ator Daniel moved to lay his resolu
tion on the table. All the senators
voted to table the resolution but eight.
Tillman stood amazed, as if he could
not believe his own ears. Frustrated
and jaded, he bounced around, seated
himself and sprang to his feet again,
quite unable to understand what was
being voted upon. He voted against
his own resolution and begged leave
to change his vote. Little attention
was paid to the request. Never before
has Ben Tillman been made to feel so
signally his own littleness. His friends
are heartily ashamed of his tirades
and even democrats were glad of the
opportunity to reprove him for his
' childish behavior.
GUILTY OF THE CRIME OF THE
CENTURY.
As yet, the country knows little of
F. Hopkinson Smith and he is known
only as a negative man to those with
whom he is best [known. But at the
annual banquet of the Confederate
Veterans’ camp, of New York City,
where Gen. Frederick D. Grant pro- ,
posed that steps be taken to make
January 19, 1907, the 100th annivers
ary of the birth of Gen. Robert E. Lee,
it was the good fortune of F. Hopkin
son Smith to be numbered as one of
the guests of that distinguished body.
The banquet order of exercises was
carefully observed until F. Hopkinson
Smith, like a frisky vixen, side-tracked
his address and made his onslaught
upon Afro-Americans. It would be
quite needless to notice the address
of Mr. Smith were it not that as a
guest of that assembly he found hon
ors thrust upon him. Just what to do
or how to wear his honors was the mo
mentous question which burdened the
soul of F. Hopkinson Smith. T v e ex-
Confederates were assembled io re
count the trials and struggles through
which they had gone during the civil
war. They had nothing to tell of the
Negro’s heroism and the glory won in
bloody conflicts fighting in defense of
national life. But there were those
whose loyalty and and patriotism re
mained unchallenged; those who
fought that the Union might live and
remain unbroken. .They were the
hundred thousand Negro soldiers who
helped to perpetuate the grandest gov
ernment on earth. Against this class
F. Hopkinson Smith raised his voice
and declared that the suffrage of the
Negro was the “greatest crime of the
century.” Two things the Afro-Amerl^
can has done which shall ever stand
as an imperishable monument to tell
lof his loyalty and devotion to his
country and his manhood. He first
proved himself the peer among sol
diers and at the close of the war he
confronted almost insurmountable ob
stacles and won his spurs as a man
and a citizen, accomplishing for him
self against tremendous odds educa
tion and wealth and the means neces
sary to assure progressiveness along
intellectual and industrial lines. No
race of people since the world began
has done better than our race, and yet
this man Smith brutally assails this
people and denounces their enfran
chisement as “the greatest crime of
the century.” Was it a crime when
the nation readmitted upon terms of
equal citizenship the very men who
had sought to destroy the govern
ment? Far less then was it a crime
when the nation was rescued and
preserved that it should in sheer grati
tude and duty confer the right of suf
frage upon those who had defended its
life. To-day the Negro stands as an
ally with the government, ready at the
first call to rescue and to save. To
day he toils amid sacrifices and suffer
ing to maintain the good name of an
honored and worthy citizen, yet like
a poor and friendless dog he is allowed
no quarters and is assailed without
mercy. F. Hopkinson Smith becomes
a party to this rejection, and in doing
so he debases himself as a southern
miscreant and an ingrate. He is not
insensible of rare benefactions of the
Negro to this country in time of war
and in peace. He is not insensible of
the trials and struggles through which
this people are now passing in order
to justly merit the guarantees of con
stitutional liberty, and he is fully con
scious of his own crime against God
and the inalienable rights of man,
when he voluntarily and maliciously
favors the abrogation of the Savior’s
sacred command of doing unto all men
as he would have them do unto him
self. Were he a Negro, standing in a
Negro's shoes, he would never consent
to the infamous deal of seeing his own
race robbed of a single blessing calcu
lated to elevate and alleviate the con
dition of an oppressed people. But as
a white man (certainly not as an hon
orable American citizen) he demands
that white men as such shall nullify
the rights of men on account of their
color, that his own race shall stoop to
the low and cowardly act of robbing
Afro-Americans of the sacred right of
suffrage, the right of protection—a
guarantee by the constitution to the
humblest white man in America. The
shame of this action rests with Smith,
for as though waking from a long
slumber in search of something to do,
yet doubting what best he may do to
aggrandize himself with what seems :
to him to be the popular vein, he i
wheels upon .the defenseless Afro-
American and in blind desperation
strikes him. In callous heartlessness
he strikes him in his weakness, and
that, too, because he dares not strike
the strong and the favored who are
admitted to a trial of a jury of his
peers. F. Hopkinson Smith, in mak
ing that shameful declaration, has
perpetrated a most shocking offense j
against humanity.

New Weather Plant.
To give to airy nothings a local,
habitation and a name Prof. Willis L.
Moore, chief of the weather bureau,
has established a special plant at
Mount Weather, Va. By means of bal
loons and kites the temperature, mois
ture and movements of the air at
great heights will be ascertained.
The absorption of solar heat by the
atmosphere will be measured by the
pyrheliometer and actinicmeter. The
dissipation of solar light and heat will
be determined by the polariscope.
The special analysis of the sunbeam
will be carried out by means of the
bolometer and spectrometer. The
electric conditions will be determined
by means of the electrometer, and the
radio activity or ionization of the air
by means of a dissipation apparatus.
To this is added appai^tus for study
ing the weather as related to the
earth’s magnetism, temperature of the
soil, and motions. All these have been
proved to have a more or less inti
mate connection with the weather.
Balloons and kites will be used to get
the temperature, moisture and move
ment of the air at great heights.
