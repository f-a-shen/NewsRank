PROE. B. K. SAMPSON.
One of Our Leading Educators, Dif
fers from Rev. Charles S. Morris.
Editor Gazette, Dear Sir: I beg
leave to offer a few comments touch
ing that remarkable letter of Rev. Dr.
Charles S. Morris, the eminent divine
of New York City. It is a high compli
ment paid Prof. B. T. Washington to
say that he wrote the speeches made
by President Roosevelt during his re
cent tour at the Florida Baptist col
lege and at Tuskegee. Mr. Washing
ton has attained greatness, but the
charge of Dr. Morris must seem to
imply that Mr. Washington is a great
er man than President Roosevelt,
while in fact the president ranks
among the greatest of the great men
of our time. When President Roose
velt made his first visit to Memphis a
noted Afro American, intending him
a welcome on behalf of his race, of
feted some very pertinent suggestions.
The president assured his audience
that to him it was a great pleasure to
listen to such an address, as well as
to earnestly consider it. We need not
doubt the effect of that address in its
relations to the future as to matters
perhaps contemplated by President
Roosevelt. Addresses delivered in the
south since that time have been
prompted by some word that fell from
the lips of rhe Negro orator; but none
will believe that the president has
used the manuscript of that colored
man. If it were true that the presi
dent does not prepare his own
speeches’. Dr. Morris might have
ground upon which to base bis charge,
but the ripe and varied knowledge that
the president has with the race and
the country absolutely refutes the
charge made by the excellent divine
of America’s great metropolis. Prof.
■Washington may have asked the pros
ident to invite the attention of the
southern people to the consideration
of certain matters in which the two
races are vitally concerned, and the
executive may have as an act of cour
tesy asked the opinion of Prof. Wash
ington in writing. Bui that the presi
dent had to depend upon Prof Wash
Ington for thoughts best suited for the
occasion is simply unreasonable. At a
banquet once given to Senator B. K.
Bruce while in Memphis, he casually
remarked to a friend who was to
speak, “I hope you will remember the
matter of which we were speaking.”
The suggestion was electrical and the
speaker before concluding his remarks
won a round of applause. Does it fol
low that; the speaker was making a
speech written by Senator Bruce? By
no means. The truth of the charge
made by Dr. Morris, if sustained at all.
must be corroborated by the secretary
of one of the two great men in ques
tion. and until this is done we are
compelled to believe that our honored
friend. Dr. Morris, is mistaken.
B. K. SAMPSON,
Memphis, Tenn.

ing repeated denials, it is well estab

Must Feed Our People
Des Moines, la.— Boarding house
keepers must furnish food to Afro
Americans as well as others on appli
cation, according to a supreme court
decision handed down recently. The
decision was given in the affirmative
of the verdict for the plaintiff in the
case of W. M. Humford vs. Humane
Officer Ed ('rawford. Humford was a
juror in a case on trial in the district
court. The jury was taken to the
Crawford boarding house on Ninth
street for meals. Crawford refused to
furnish Humford meals. He brought
a civil action and secured a verdict
for damages. Judge Howe heard the
case in the lower court.

Will Build Panama Canal.
Washington. D. C.—The Panama
canal will be built by the labor of
West Indian Negroes, chiefly’ because
of the objection of labor unions. Chair
man Shouts has abandoned the pro
posed employment of foreign laborers
and will make no contract for the de
livery on the isthmus of large numbers
of Chinese, Japanese and Italian work
men, as was contemplated several
weeks ago. It has been found that
enough strong, husky West Indians
voluntarily drift into Panama in
search of work to provide all laborers
required on the canal.
The Civil Rights Law Good
Pittsburg. Pa. —Attorney James J.
O'Malley, referee in the case of Thos.
Hundley against Edward and Lewis
Jifkins, proprietors of the “White

all citizens. Hundley claimed that
when he had been refused a drink in
the defendants’ hotel because he was
an Afro-American the law had been
violated. Referee O'Malley held the
plaintiff had suffered SIOO damages
for the humiliation be was subjected
to. Hundley was a miner and form
