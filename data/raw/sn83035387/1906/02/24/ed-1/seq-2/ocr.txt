A POWERFUL PLEA AGAINST UN
LAWFUL KILLING.
Dr. A. B. Curry, a southern white di
vine, recently delivered an eloquent
sermon against the unlawful taking of
human life. Among other things he
said:
“During the past week a colored
man on one of the busy streets of our
city (Memphis) found himself suspect
ed of dishonesty and, fearing trouble,
he ran. This man was punished by a
private citizen and shot to death, as
he would a friendless dog. I know not
that this instance shows a worse con
dition in our community than in some
other section of our country, but sure
ly it is sufficient to cause serious re
flection and to make appropriate our
study of the text this morning: ‘Who
so sheddeth man’s blood, by man shall
his blood be shed, for in the image of
God, made He man.’ The saddest
sight on earth is a slain man. Even a
slain animal is an affecting sight, but
transcending all this, a slain man
whose soul is hastened into eternity
unbidden by the Creator and whose
body was temple of God, now despoil
ed and stricken to the earth. Hence,
to take human life unlawfully is a
crime against God and man. It is to
strike at the foundation of human so
ciety, which is based on the sacred
ness of human life. It is to take from
a man what man can never give back —
his life. It is to lay one’s hand ruth
lessly on God’s image and despoil it.
It is to bring upon oneself God's
curse, even though he escapes the ven
geance of man. Were I guilty of the
blood of my fellow-man I would fear
most of all the mark of God’s wrath,
the mark which He placed on the first
murderer and caused him to cry out in
anguish: ‘My punishment is greater
than I can bear.’ 1 believe He places
a mark upon every such man—murder
is the hardest of all crimes to conceal,
because God is exposing it. It is this
which banishes happiness from the
murderer and fills his life with terror
and unrest. It brings a curse upon the
community which tolerates it —it is to
revert to barbarism, for it is a mark of
barbarism. And yet there is not an
enlightened Christian country in the
world where human life is so cheap as
in the United States. Indeed, there
are more homicides in the United
States than in any other land in all
Christendom. It is our shame and a
blot upon our civilization, and if un
punished it brings God’s displeasure
on a country and its people. A land
full of blood is polluted in His sight
and an abomination to Him. Unaveng
ed human blood cries to heaven as the
blood of Abel and brings a curse on
the land that refuses to hear that cry.
We cannot sit in our homes and
churches with silent lips and folded
hands and hope to escape' the wrath of
a just God while human blood stains
the stones of our levees and thorough
fares.”
This appeal of Dr. Curry comes to
every loyal citizen who regards the
sanctity of law and the sacredness of
human life. It is an appeal for the
suppression of lawlessness and mob
violence. It is an appeal to all Chris
tians and law-abiding citizens to re
gard more earnestly the sacredness of
human life, to abhor with all the soul
the lawless shedding of human blood,
and it demands that our courts shall
do their full duty promptly and in the
fear of God. It comes as an appeal to
the fathers and mothers and teachers
and ministers everywhere in America
to unite in purging the land of inno
cent blood.
“THE CLANSMAN.’’
When Tom Dixon hatched up that
infamous scandal, so named Clans
man, and gave it out to the public, he
conceived in mind a crafty way of re
alizing a good sum of money as a re
lief from that sort of physical toil to
which his course nature seems best
adapted. It is not that the book has
any genuine merit in itself, nothing
from which the American youth can
derive any real benefit or gather any
high inspiration, nerving the soul to
lofty purpose, but because it is sensa
tional and defamatory and appeals to
the baser passions of a certain ele
ment that find food for a morbid and
immoral bent of the mind Already
it is in evidence that the better class
of the southern mind is incensed that
auch a production should be placed
upon the market So repulsive and
degenerating is the whole drift of the
thought conceived by Dixon that those
who have perused the work have de
nounced it without reserve and brand
it as a silly and idle conception. Very
true, the work is perused by the curi
ous and reading public, and white
young men who have recently come
from college, as well as the more pro.
gressive and thoughtful, have given
their opinion that such a book should
not be allowed in the homes of the peo
ple. Some three decades or more ago
• email worK a physiology, was pre-
pared and offered for sale in the state
of New York. It was a valuable com
pendium of facts and contained infor
mation of rare value, such as could
not be disputed, but so startling, so
nauseating and repulsive was the sub
ject matter treated that the legislature
of that state imposed a severe penalty
upon all publishers who might give out
such a publication. We do not wonder
that steps are being taken to suppress
the publication of a work so immoral
and detrimental to public interest as
the Clansman is so well known to be.
Like its author, the book is a mischief
breeder and reflects the tendency of a
vile and vicious nature and altogether
too pernicious to deserve the time
given to its reading. The Paducah
Evening Sun, one of the leading jour
nals of Kentucky, writes of the Clans
man as follows:
“The Clansman” did not create
much of a furor until it got up to Lex
ington, Ky. There it aroused so much
feeling that a bill was the next day
introduced in the legislature to pre
vent the presentation of such plays in
the future. The Lexington Herald
quotes the following as some of the
expressions heard:
“If “The Clansman' had been pro
duced 30 years ago tfyere would have
been another war.
“Splendid inspiration for a lynching
party.
“When the play is produced in the
south somebody in the audience Is go
ing to take a shot at that man playing
the lieutenant governor. He plays
the part too well for safety.”
After the presentation a prominent
club passed the following:
“Resolved, that the department of
current events of the Woman’s club
of central Kentucky deeply deplore
the presentation of ‘The Clansman’
and all such plays which arouse sec
tional feeling.”
The Montgomery Advertiser, Ala.,
observes: z
“The play is bad, top, bottom and
sides, and it hurts. It is false, mis
chievous and exaggerated as Uncle
Tom’s Cabin. It should be denounced.
It should not be tolerated.”
Now, after all these years of strug
gle among Americans for better con
ditions, from the bloody contest of the
fathers to throw off the yoke of British
tyranny and to assume their rightful
place among the nations of the earth,
there comes a man robed in tainted
garments, given to strategy and spoils,
plotting treason and seeking to plunge
the new republic into domestic dissen
sion and civil strife. The Clansman
must be repudiated.

DOUGLASS-DUNBAR.
A Proposition to Erect a Monument
That Will be a Lasting Tribute—
Danpy’s Suggestion.
Columbus, O. —A commendable ef
fort is that which proposes to preserve
the memory of Frederick Douglass
and Paul Laurence Dunbar by erecting
a suitable memorial. In every city of
the country, of any size, there is a
memorial erected to the memory of
some white man who has won distinc
tion in either war or peace, but the
distinguished dead of the Negro race
of America are forgotten, so far as
a memorial to their memory is con
cerned. This desire to preserve the
memory of Frederick Douglass and
Paul Laurence Dunbar to the genera
tions yet to come may be attributed to
the Douglass-Dunbar Memorial as
sociation. Hon. John C. Dancy, re
corder of deeds, says: "I am in per
fect sympathy with the object of the
great undertaking, and think the me
morial should be here (Washington)
at Cedar Hill and that statues of both
men should be erected in a great me
morial building on that site where all
visitors to the capitol could behold
them.” And similar sentiments are
expressed by many others in different
parts of the country.
A memorial commission, with whom
will rest the power to determine what
form the memorial shall take, wheth
er a memorial building, or a monu
ment, and wl^re to be located, has
been named consisting of Hon. John
C. Dancy, Prof. Kelly Miller and Mrs.
Mary Church Terrell, of Washington,
D. C.; Bishop Grant, of Indianapolis;
Bishop Walters, of Jersey City: Gen.
Robert Smalls, of North Carolina; Dr.
Booker T. Washington, of Tuskegee;
Hon. Charles W. Anderson, of New
York; Rev. R. C. Hansom, of Boston;
Prof. W. E. Dußois, of Atlanta; Prof.
J. McHenry Jones, of Institute, W.
Va.; John Mitchell, of Richmond, Va.;
J. C. Napier, of Nashville; Dr. Jorden,
of Louisville; Hon. Harry C. Smith, of
Cleveland, and Hon. George W. Hayes,
of Cincinnati.
Hon. J. C. Napier, president of the
Penny Savings, bank, of Nashville,
has been naqied as treasurer, and
Ralph W. Tyler, of the Ohio State
Journal, Columbus, 0., secretary. It
is proposed that this memorial,
whether a memorial building or a
statue, shall be a lasting evidence of
the race’s appreciation of these men
and an epoch In the race s advance
ment, and that when it is erected, its
dedication be made the greatest epoch
in the history of the race since the
emancipation. Every Negro man. wo
man and child in America should lend
an effort to the consummation of this
great object. The secretary will ac
knowledge promptly all contributions,
and it is Intended that every donor be
sent a souvenir certificate, setting
forth his contribution, which may be
preserved. The investment of SIOO,-
000 in a lasting monument to
Douglass, that heroic figure of our
earlier days who blazed the way for
the race's present advancement, and
Paul Laurence Dunbar, the poet,
whose genius was made possible by
such men as Douglass, would be a
mighty incentive to the young of this
and future generations. Do you k*.e
your race, and your men and women
who have achieved something for the
race? If so, now is the time to show
it, for too long has it been said that
the race is unappreciative of the race's

PROBERS REPORT.
The Armstrong Committee Suggests
Remedial Legislation to Protect
the Policy Holders.
New York, Feb. 23.—The committee
appointed at the last session of the
New York legislature to investigate
life insurance made its report Thurs
day. The report embraces a long re
view of the testimony taken by the
committee and its recommendations
and conclusions as to remedial legisla
tion. In addition there is a chapter
devoted to the state insurance de
partment in which the committee de
clares that the superintendent of the
department has had ample power to
ascertain the transactions of insur
ance companies, but the supervision
by the department has not proved a
sufficient protection against extrava
gance and maladministration.
No substantial amplification of the
powers of the department seems nec
essary according to the' committee,
which holds that most of the evils
which have been disclosed by the in
vestigation would have been impossi
ble had there been a vigorous per
formance of the duties already laid
upon the insurance department.
The remedial legislation recom
mended by the company provides for
the safeguarding of the rights of pol
icyholders in mutual companies in the
election of directors; recommends that
stock companies be given authority to
retire their stock and become mutual
companies, but that such mutualiza
tion shall not be compulsory; various
regulations are urged to prevent un
wise investments and improper syndi
cate operations; the sale of prohibited
securities within five years is advo
cated. A recommendation for the lim
iting of new business to $250,000,000 a
year is made; the committee con
demns the contributions by insurance
companies for political purposes, lob
bying is condemned and the wisdom
of economical management is urged.
The committee says the accounts of
the Mutual Life Insurance Co. should
be thoroughly examined in order that
the extent to which moneys have been
misapplied and the responsibility for
any misapplication which may be
shown may be determined.
Concerning the New York Life in
surance Co., the committee found that
its transactions with Andrew Hamil
ton showed extraordinary abuses, and
that the statement sent from Paris by
Hamilton was without suitable specifi
cations.
In taking up the Equitable Life As
surance Society, the committee tells
of the dissensions last February which
resulted in the, reorganization of that
society and in the disclosures which
brought about the legislative inquiry.
The paynient of $20,000 a year to
Senator Depew by the Equitable, the
committee holds, was not warranted,
the testimony as to the services ren
dered by Senator Depew not appearing
to be sufficient for such payment. The
committee also sets forth that it does
not appear what services were render
ed by ex-Senator Hill, who was paid
$5,000 a year. In justice to Mr. Hill,
the committee says it was not able to
get his testimony upon this subject
because he was too ill to appear.
The committee finds that in spite of
the irregularities shown there is no
reason to question the solvency of the
Mutual Life Insurance Co., the New
York Life Insurance Co. nor the Equi
table Life Assurance Society.

Thirty Men in the Carnegie Steel
Plant at Duquesne. Pa.. Are In
jured by an Explosion.
Pittsburg, Feb. 23.—Thirty persons
were hurt, three of whom will die, by
the explosion Thursday of a 200-pound
charge of dynamite, which was struck
by a steam shovel at the Carnegie
Steel Co.’s plant at Duquesne. The
steam shovel was removing a slag pile
that had been previously broken up
with the explosive. During the break
ing up process one of the charges of
dynamite was lost, and it is this one
that was exploded yesterday.
The steam shovel was plowing its
way into the slag when there was a
mighty roar, the ground trembled and
the shovel structure was lifted into the
air. parting and breaking up and fall
ing to the ground a mass of splintered
timber. Sharp flinty pieces of the slag
were scattered in every direction and
added to these were pieces of splint
ered timber from the shattered shovel
which were hurled among a hundred
Hungarian laborers, by the bursting of
the boiler.
When the rain of timber and slag
had ceased, the injured lay about the
ground, some calling for help, while
ethers attempted to assist their neigh
bors.
