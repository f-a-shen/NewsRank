The acute condition which the la
bor problem is assuming in the south
1® u condition the black workingman

THE CUBAN REVOLT.
The real cause of the Cuban insur
rection or revolution now on crops
out in the following excerpt from the
letter to the St. Louis (Mo.) Globe
Democrat, written and sent, by an
American resident of the island ever
since the Spanish-American war, in
which he was a participant. Said the
writer:
“Under (Spanish) despotism, the
blacks quietly accepted their low
caste position, but, as they are nu
merous, although a minority here, as
individuals have more physical
strength than native whites, possess
more natural intelligence than the
blacks of the states, and not so much
less than their white neighbors here,
and especially as they furnished a
much larger proportion than the
whites of the soldiers who fought for
liberty, they resist class distinction
against them under the republic. The
whites have been growing aggressive
in this respect latterly, and much dis
content among the blacks is the re
sult, and they preponderate in the in
surrection army of to-day.”
The writer's prejudice is easily no
ticeable in his comparisons of the
“blacks" of Cuba and "The (United)
States,” and the former with the
whites of the island. The fact is
there is little or no difference in the
.“natural intelligence” of white and
colored Cubans. The latter, however,
are different from Afro-Americans in
that they will not quietly submit to
mistreatment of any kind, especially
that which is resultant from a color
line condition created with the assist
ance of the half American baked Cu
ban president, since the close of the
Spanish-American war by prejudiced
Americans who settled in Cuba since
that conflict. The Palma administra
tion, we sincerely trust, will be de
posed and another secured that is
more truly Cuban—free of the bane
ful influence of the silly American col
or or race prejudice.
GANS-NELSON BRUTALISM.
The Cleveland Gazette. Ohio, pub
lished and edited by Hon. Harry C.
Smith, is among our most brilliant,
fearless and interesting Negro ex
changes. Editor Smith is not of that
type of journalists, whose fearless
ness prompts him to write about mat
ters of public concern, because of an
opportunity for dash and recklessness
in defense of a given question. The
editorials of The Gazette are usually
thoughtful, clear-cut and direct to the
end, contemplated by the mind's eye,
and just conservative enough to show
a disposition for fair play to his op
ponents.
A large number of our race journals
have simply gone into ecstacy over
the acknowledged victory of the noted
Negro pugilist, Joe Gans, in his re
cent fight with "Battling” Nelson. The
Gazette takes a thoughtful view of
the situation and tacitly asks itself
the question, whether in the brutal ex
hibition of human conduct between
Gans and Nelson, if there has been
demonstrated any special prowess
worthy of racial pride, other than
physical endurance?
The duelistic combat with deadly
weapons .have long since been re
garded as too brutal for honorary
mention. In our judgment, the duel
ist and pugilist is one and the same.
The former shoots or cuts to kill, the
later aims to strike some vital organ,
most likely to produce instant insen
sibility from which death may follow.
One is voluntary murder, the other
involuntary, and both should be
classed among crimes of premeditated
murder, and therefore, should be pro
hibited by law. —Pensacola (Fla.)
Sentinel.

THE CUBAN REVOLT.
After a protracted conference with
Secretary of War Taft, Acting Secre
tary of State Bacon and Secretary of
the Navy Bonaparte, President Roose
velt addressed an important communi
cation to Cuba and arranged to send
Secretary Taft and Bacon to that
island to make thorough investigation
of conditions there and lend their in
fluence to restore peace.
The only results thus far of Presi
dent Palma’s order for the suspension
of hostilities have been that liberal
leaders, who hitherto have had every
reason for anticipating arrest, are cir
culating openly in Havana again and
even conferring with members of the
government with regard to peace.
Points of special and present consid
eration in the peace negotiations are
the fixing of a neutral zone and camp
ing places for the insurgent forces
and the supplying of them with food,
which it seems to be expected will
have to be done by the government.
While no acceptable basis of peace
appears to have been suggested by
either side, the impression is general
that Secretary Taft will take up the
work of peace making with small con
sideration for past grievances, the
main idea being rather constructive
measures for the future.
The newest feature of the revolu
tion is the appearance of Americans
from the Isle of Pines complaining
that officials of the courts of the Isle
of Pines are searching the homes of
all Americans for firearms and seiz
ing all arms which may be found.
