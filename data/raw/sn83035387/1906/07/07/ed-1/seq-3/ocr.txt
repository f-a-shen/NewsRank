.“If I were not in a' Presbyterian
church I would say that 1 would bet
the white, people cf the United States
that the colored man will g^t well.”
Thus spoke Booker-T. Washington in
his address at the Calvary Presbyter
ian church at the conclusion of the
following anecdote: “A colored sold
ier lay wounded on a battlefield of the
civil war. Thetchaplain went to hiAi
and asked him if there was any word
he wanted to send to his loved ones
at home. The soldier answered: “Put
yo ahm undah ma neck, now lift me
haid jes’ little, now tak dat five-dol
lar bill fom undah ma shut an hole it
up befo ma ahs so dat Ah cain see it
plain.' This was done and the sold
ier, summoning all his remaining
strength, raised himself on his elbow,
and, shaking his fist feebly in the
chaplain's face, cried: 'Chaplain. Ah'll
jes’ bet you dat five-dollar bill dat dis
niggahll git well.’” From this story
Mr. Washington drew the conclusion
that the Negro will eventually rise
above his present level and be the

States. He outlined the work being
