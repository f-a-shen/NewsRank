Comment and Suggestive Thought.
V. 36. “Then.” About midnight
’Cometh Jesus with them.” Jesus with
the eleven apostles. Judas having left
the company. “Gethsemane.” Ap
parently this garden was owned by
some friend of Jesus, since we read
that “Jesus ofttimes resorted thither
with his disciples. “Saith . . .Sit
ye here.” Jesus left eight of his
friends near the garden gate, that
they might watch against surprise by
the enemy. “While I go and pray"
Prayer was the very atmosphere of
Jesus’ life.
V. 37. “Peter and the two sons of
Zebedee.” These three —Peter, James
and John —best understood Jesus, and
had been his chosen companions on
two earlier occasions. “Began to be
sorrowful.” Sore amazed (Mark). It
was as if he were stunned by extreme
amazement.
V. 38. “My soul is exceedingly sor
rowful.” A proverbial expression of
severe suffering. “Even unto death."
That is. he feared the spiritual agony
would be too great for the physical
nature to endure. “Tarry ye here.”
Jesus wished to be alone with his Fa
ther, yet was so truly human that he
longed for human sympathy. “Watch
with me.” He would have them keep
awake from sympathy with him, watch
against any intrusion upon his com
munion with his Father, and also
watch against the danger which
threatened them as well as him
self.
V. 39. “Went a little further.” Luke
says he went ‘about a stones cast.”
“Fell on his face and prayed."
Kneeled to pray; then in anguish of
spirit, utterly prostrated himself. “Say
ing.” Compare the three records —
Mark 14:36; Luke, 22:42, and the Les
son Text. “O my Father.” He ad
dressed God as “my Father,” and
^herein shows us the meaning and
the power of prayer. “If it be pos
sible.” If man s redemption can be
accomplished in any other way. “This
cup.” A common figurative expression
to denote the lot or portion assigned
one by God. “Not as I will, but
as thou wilt.” He was not merely
willing to submit to God's will, but
prayed for it; heartily desired that
the all-wise, all-loving will be ac
complished at any cost.

Came to the three whom he had take<?
with him intb the garden. “Finde^
them sleeping.” Though they love©

his feelings sufficiently to overcome
drowsiness. “Saith unto Peter.” He
singled out the one who had been
spokesman when recently they all de
clared themselves ready to die with
their Master. (Matt. 26:33-35). “Could
ye not watch with me one hour?”
Jesus’ heart was sorely wounded, not
merely because they were so cold
toward him, but because of the peril
mto which they had drifted by yield
ing to their drowsiness.
V. 41. “Watch and pray.” They, and
we, should watch because the great
adversary of souls is very wise and

our own strength we are powerless to
resist him. “Ye enter not into temp

was the great temptation to deny and
desert Jesus.
V. 42. “Prayed, saying,” etc. The
slight change in the words he uttered
in his second prayer is noteworthy,
for it gives evidence that he has been
strengthened and is more calm.
V. 45. “Sleep on now.” The time
for watching and prayer was past.
V. 46. “Rise, let us be going. ’ The
approaching torches effectually roused
the slumbering disciples, and they
arose to go with Jesus, not to hide
from, but to meet, the company com

New York Girl Now Lady Paget.
There are now ten Lady Pagets in
Great Britain, the latest being the
wife of Gen. Paget, who has just been
knighted. She was Miss Minnie,
daughter of Mrs. Paran Stevens, of
New York. Her first appearance in
London society was made a good
many years ago, when her beauty and
vivacity caused quite a sensation.
Bird’s Unbroken Flight.
In one unbroken nocturnal flight the
European bird known as the north
ern blue throat has been proved to
travel from central Africa to the Ger
man ocean, a distance of 1,690 miles,
making the journey in nine hours.
