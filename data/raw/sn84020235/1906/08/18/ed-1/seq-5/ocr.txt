THE PROFESSIONAL WORLD.
In the issue of August 3rd, this little
two by four sheet, called a newspaper,
is still on the war path.
He made a savage attack upon the
administration —he attempted to play
to the rabble, then with the venom of
a reptile, he strikes at the Grand Mas
ter and the Grand Secretary; again he
strikes at the Grand Officers and says
they are making every possible effort
to be re-elected on some constitutional
limit. This at once shows the deep
seated evil that is in the man Logan.
He pays Dr. J. T. Castons and Dr. O.
C. Queen a high compliment, but al
most in the same breath, throws a dag
ger into Prof. Coleman, of Columbia,
Mo.
We doubt very much whether there
is a principal in the state of Missouri
that does not feel a fear when this
man makes his appearance in their
schools. He is known to wander from
school to school like the Devil, seeking
whom he may displace from their
school. In our judgment, the present
administration has done well, with
perhaps two exceptions, and we be
lieve if they have done well, they
should be re-elected and the delegates
to the Grand Lodge ought to remem
ber who this man Logan is.
Large armies move slowly, so has
the Grand Officers of the State of Mis
souri done; every time they moved
they done something for the order. So
let well enough alone.

APPROPRIATION FOR PRESIDENT
ROOSEVELT’S TRAVELING
EXPENSES.
The special bill appropriating $25,-
000 for the coming fiscal year to pay
the president’s traveling expenses and
making a permanent charge on the
treasury for this purpose passed the
house, a number of Democrats
voting with the majority for it. This
differs from the sundry civil amend
ment killed on a point of order in
that it leaves to the president’s dis
cretion who shall accompany him on
his trips. Underwood of Alabama and
several other Democrats opposed the
measure on grounds of expediency and
constitutionality. Cockran of New
York in defense of the item eloquent
ly praised Roosevelt’s speeches as
“the most potent factors in accom
plishing in legislation the purpose of
Democratic policies.” The measure
was opposed in the senate by leading
Democrats, but was passed by a strict
party vote. The days of Jeffersonian
simplicity are passing away and some
may agree with Senator Bailey of
Texas, who stated, “that President
Roosevelt costs ^fiore and is worth
less than any President we ever had.”
