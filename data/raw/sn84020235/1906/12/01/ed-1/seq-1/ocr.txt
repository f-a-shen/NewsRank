At the last regular meeting of the
Central Protective League, at their
hall, 2727 Franklin avenue, the ques
tion discussed was the discharge of
the enlisted men of B, C and D com
panies of the Twenty-fifth infantry,
U. S. A.
Mr. Collins, an ex-member of Com
pany B, explained the feeling exhibit
ed by the people of the southern army
post towns against the Colored "boys
in blue.”
Mr. C. H. Tandy expressed a fore
ful opposition to the attitude assumed
by the president and proposed a reso
lution protesting against the very
sweeping order of discharge without
honor.
Mr. George W. Boldrew advocated
a calm and thoughtful consideration
of the matter and counseled modera
tion.
Mr. Thompson requested a more
thorough investigation of the facts as
given by the press, and suggested that
a closer’ scrutiny of the order of dis
charge might show’ that a false con
struction had been placed upon the
president's meaning.
Mr. Moore w r as not sure that the dis
charge was purely a race issue, nor
did he believe that the president had
changed in his attitude toward the
Negro,

Olive Leaf Drill Company of the S. M.
T. Met and Installed Their
Officers.
Last Tuesday night at 8 o’clock the
Olive Leaf Drill Corps at its regular
meeting installed their officers. This
company is one year old, and has ex
celled all other lady drill corps that
have been in existence one year, and
vanquished every company that they
have met in a competitive drill. After
excelling all companies in this city
they went to Lexington, Ky., last July
and gave an exhibition drill, which
caused the crack drill company of
Louisville to back out. Having re
turned from there they went to Colum
bia, Mo., last August, and won the
admiration of the order of the U. B. F.
and S. M. T., and were presented with
§SO by the Grand Lodge, $lO from
the Grand Temple, and won the prize
offered.
Mrs. Mary Monroe is without a
doubt the best drill mistress that we
have ever seen. Her commands are
clear and distinct, and she gives the
members time to imbibe the intended
movements, which are excellent and
prompt. The officers and members
love and respect their commander.
At a late hour Sister Maria Üband
introduced J. W. Wheeler, who in
stalled the officers, paying them a
high compliment for their many victo
ries that they have achieved from
time to time.
The two little mascots w’ere pres
ent —Cecilia Brunner and Gertrude
Mahoney.
Mrs. Jessie Miller, the secretary,
was on hand, and the reporter was

Everyone enjoyed themselves until

homes.

Resolution of Central Protective
League.
2727 Franklin Avenue.
• Nov. 25, 1906.
We the officers and members of
Central Protective League, in regular
session assembled, do resolve that:
Whereas, The president of the
United States has by his act of dis
charging without honor enlisted men
of Companies B, C and D, Twenty-fifth
infantry, thus debarring those men
from all the rights of suffrage and
from all civil 'employment under the
United States government; and,
Whereas, The regulations of the
United States army do guarantee a
trial by court-martial to all enlisted
men against whom charges are pre
ferred ; and,
Whereas, Such trial by court-mar
tial was not granted the enlisted men
of Companies B, C and D, Twenty
fifth infantry; therefore, be it
Resolved, That we, as true and loyal
citizens of the United States, do pro
test against such high-handed injustice
by our president; and we, as Ameri
can citizens, will appeal through our
representatives in congress for uie
restoration of all the rights of those
brave soldiers who have carried the
flag of our great nation to honor and
victory on every battlefield contested
by them.
Notwithstanding the president’s re
marks of a year ago that ‘the Door of
Hope should not be closed against
any man,” still he with his unlimited
power has closed the door of hope
against the Twenty-fifth infantry. Not
only has he closed the door, but he
has barred it forever.
C. H. TANDY, Chairman,
E. H. TAYLOR.
J. W. WHEELER.
CHAS. M. FRANKLIN.
JAS. W. HUTT, Secretary.

THE GRAND LEADER BOYS
Give Their First Annual Ball, Which
Was a Grand Success.
The Grand Leader boys came before
the public in their first annual ball
at Douglass hall Wednesday evening,
November 28, 1906.
The hall was beautifully decorated
with the colors of the Leader, which
were blue and white.
Just over the musicians the name of
the club, “Grand Leader Boys,” was
displayed in gorgeous letters, around
which were hung beautiful flowers.
The musicians were almost hidden
from view by immense palms and
beautiful draperies, to which still
more beauty was added by the reflec
tions from the large colored lanterns
on either end. From every window in
the hall long draperies of blue and
white were hung. Never before in
the history of St. Louis have the
younger boys made such a showing as
on last Wednesday evening. The
boys have made their reputation, and
the next essential feature is to re
tain the summit to which they have
ascended.
It was intended that every one
should be attired in evening dress, and
this rule was strictly adhered to,
vith the exception of a few cases; and
in such cases they should take notice
and govern themselves accordingly in
the near future.
Carriages surrounded Douglass
building from an early hour in the
evening until past midnight.
The officers of the club are as fol
lows: Jesse Raybon, president; Da
vid Walker, vice-president; Frank
Wilson, secretary; James Formento,
treasurer; Leslie Taylor, manager.
Members: Thomas Brown, William
Williams, William Wallace, Harry
Bailey, Leon Simpson, Oscar Blanton
and Norman Lankford.
The dance program was followed
throughout the course of the evening,
and varied only when the flashlight
picture was taken by Mr. W. H. Sex
ton at 11 o'clock. At 12 o’clock the
grand march was played, which was
led by James Harris, our famous
young violinist, after which every one
repaired to the dining room tor sup
per.
