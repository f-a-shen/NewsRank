THE APPEAL IN A MUDDLE.
The Commercial Appeal, of Mem
phis, Tenn., seems just now to be
having a sorry time in the courts de
fending itself against a suit for dam
ages. The present company has not
Btood for fair play and a liberal show
ing to all classes. The Appeal has
with unscrupulous readiness stood out
against the Afro-American upon the
merest charge of criminality. Ru
mors of misdemeanors and misrepre
sentation have been issued again and
again with a dogged determination to
humiliate, degrade and repress the
race even at the sacrifice of truth and
honor. With a presumption and bold
ness to blot out Negro manhood and
to stifle the better aspirations of the
race, the Appeal has sought by threat
and intimidation to checkmate the
earnest endeavor of the Afro-Ameri
can in asserting his personal and po
litical freedom as a man, a citizen and
a brother. Trained to the practice of
striking Indiscriminately it very re
cently published in its columns a
slanderous article against one of the
leading white ministers of Memphis.
The entire congregation resolved at
once to vindicate the good name of
their beloved and honored pastor, and
a persistent and uncompromising fight
between the company and the church
Is the result. Retraction and the
refutation of detraction are the meth
ods resorted to by the defendants. The
fates are against them and there is
no better time than now that the Ap
peal and those of its kind who train
in the school of slander, abuse and
foul misrepresentation, should call a
halt. Villiany and inhuman onslaught
upon any people are at no time ex
cusable and should not be tolerated in
the midst of an enlightened and Chris
tian nation. A fair deal to all men
should be the tocsin of the religious
world and without such a deal, the
proudest and strongest of earth is
base, corrupt, and full of dead men's
bones. The American churches even
yet have a lesson to teach and the
press of the country is called upon to
heed the admonition of the solemn in
junction of shielding the innocent of
all races and rallying to the support
of right and justice against wrong,
maliciousness and high-handed wick
edness.
VARDAMAN’S COWARDLY ACT.
As governor of a great state this
man Vardaman has shown himself to
be the most beastly and treacherous
of all beings existing in human form.
Holding the highest office in the gift
the people of Mississippi, he abused
It in striking down and beating a
poor, defenseless Afro-American with
no right nor authority of law. That
the man was a convict and working
around the executive mansion was no
reason that he should be made the
victim of personal spleen and vin
dictive wrath- The man was engaged
in 'shining the governor's shoes. He
was charged with impudence because
he sought to exonerate himself from
misrepresentation, and at once “his
majesty” in his inconceivable immen
aity kicked and cuffed the Afro-Ameri
can, and seizing a broom stick, ad
ministered (as he tells it) a very
sound thrashing. Like a cowardly
miscreant, Vardaman did what he
dared not to do under different cir.
cumsiances. Had the Afro-American
an equal chance with himself, the
governor would hardly have risked
himself in the single combat. But
acting under the shield of a powerful
state administration, he ignored his
oath of obligation and like some un
tamed native of the forest makes a
despised Negro the prey of unbridled
ferocity. Vardaman may boast of his
proud Saxon ancestry, but his conduct
< c this case was base, unmagnani
mous inhuman. un-American and un
beco^ing a high minded gentleman of
any raoe, Vet the deed though loath
some and foul, bespeaks the vicious
propensity of the man. But Varda
man is under oath to maintain the
law, yet he violates the law and claims
the right to hold his place as gover
nor of the state. He punishes crimi
nals for offense against the peace and
authority of the law, yet he offers in
his own conduct the example of a
law-breaker and a criminal. What an
anomaly indeed! He joined the law
breakers in their guilty encroach
ments upon the laws and statutes of
Mississippi and stands today a tried
and condemned convict in the minds
of the people. He bandied words with
a supposed inferior and placed himself
upon the plane of a common wrangler:
then finding himself unequal to the
emergency at once resorted to brutal
Intimidation. Not long since this
disciple of the law sallied forth from
the capital of his state with chartered
trains trumpeting his determination
to defeat the object of the mob and
to save Mississippi from the shame of
dishonor and bloody lawlessness. The
country applauded the act and be
lieved the governor to be at least a
man of courage, if not sincere. But
it was only a matter of merest bluster
intended as a bid for public confidence
and promotion. In furtherance of his
design to gain confidence and a higher
trust, he recommends to the legisla
ture of the state a discontinuance of
the usual appropriations to be made
for the support of Afro-American
schools. Wily as a crafty fox, he re
sorts to strategy and spoils only to be
regarded as the coming man for a
seat in the nation’s senate. Varda
man can never be regarded as being
more than an average man, but he is
a shrewd politician and seeks by noise
and bluster to keep himself before the
people. Now pray what has he done
that renders him worthy of high com
mendation or that places him in line
with the strong men of his state? The
people are every day gathering unto
themselves a broader and more en
during intelligence and the time has
passed when they shall be beguiled in
to false and spurious ideas as to the
duties and obligations of true states
manship. He will never again fill the
governor's chair and he will never
reach the United States senate. Stand
ing where he now stands, his crime
as a lawbreaker is the unpardonable
sin.

servant of Gen. Robert E. Lee, of the

letters and papers which proved that

for many years. A few years after
the close of the civil war Mrs. Wash
ington came to Philadelphia to take
care of her son, who was seriouslj’ ill.
She expected to return to the south
and take up her duties in the Lee
family, but never left this city. She
described Gen. Lee as a perfect
gentleman.

London, England.—Baron Suyemat
su, former Japanese minister of the
interior, who is on his way home,
writes to the Times, from Port Said, a
bitter complaint. He says that him
self and three of his compatriots, one
of whom had been naval attache at
London, and the other a professor
holding degrees from the’ universities
of Liepzig and Oxford, were kept at
the bottom of the table on board the
North German Lloyd steamer Zieten,
while passengers who came on board
after them were given higher seats.
The Chinese minister at London, the
baron says, was treated with the
same discourtesy, with the result that
all had to demand to be seated at
separate tables. Baron Suyematsu
protests strongly against the treat
ment accorded himself and his com
panions, which was simply, he says,
because they were Japanese.

Refuses to Fight Lightweight Cham
pion Under Any Conditions.
Los Angeles. Cal. —Jimmy Britt,
when questioned as to whether he
was willing to meet Joe Gans, replied
emphatically that under no circum
stances could he be drawn into a
match with the champion lightweight.
“My ambition is to meet Nelson and
regain the championship," said Britt.
“The only reason that will cause me
to fight before that time would be a
chance to remove any possible ob
stacle that Nelson may say lies in the
way of a match. I do not consider
Gans in the light of an obstacle, and
absolutely will not fight him. 1 beat
him once, although he gained the de
cision on a foul. Besides, Nelson
would demand weight conditions that
Gans would not consent to should any
talk of a fight between the two arise.
Nelson and McGovern have both
promised me a match, but I believe
Nelson will use every pretext to get
out of meeting me. That is the rea
son I am unwilling to meet anyone
who may stand in the way of a match
with Nelson.”
