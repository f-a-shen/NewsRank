Self and race respecting Afro-
Americans can no longer patronize the
National Cash Register Co., of Day
ton, O. The company has drawn a
color or race line which prevents

Indeed, has discharged a large number

LET PATTERSON ANSWER.
Although the masses of our people
of this country are accredited as being
constant in toil and as having done
marvelously well during the days of
their freedom, yet it is argued by their
enemies that among them are more
vagrants, as well as more prisoners in
the penitentiary, than the whites. We
ask, then, why is this? Enjoying only
forty years of freedom, the race has,
as a matter of course, been compelled
to depend upon the white man and the
government for employment. Until
they had first improved their condition
as freed men it was hardly to be ex
pected that they were other than de
pendents. But from their very begin
ning hs freed men there has been a
disposition on the part of a large
prejudiced class to snub the race and
to deny them employment unless it is
of the most menial kind, and even
then when these menial occupations
afforded constant profit, the colored
man has been almost invariably elimi
nated for a white substitute. Not that
the Afro-American is not competent,
but that the compensation is too much
for a Negro. Being thus supplanted
again and y again, we find reasonable
cause why we have many vagrants
and idlers south and north. A thou
sand chances are afforded to the white
man to secure employment to the Ne
gro's one. Whites denounce the Afro-
American as a vagrant and a rogue,
while in the meantime they force him
to that condition. Hard pressed, day
after day, he unwillingly accepts
whatever employment he can get, in
order to earn bread. But he finds
none and he must rove about as a
tramp, seeking for an opportunity to
earn something rather than suffer and
starve. The great firms are ever
ready and willing to promote white la
borers and encourage them in the
pursuit of an honest livelihood. The
white laborers after awhile agree to
strike for higher wages. The Afro-
American sees his opportunity and
ofttimes becomes a strike breaker. He
is now in demand —he wants employ
ment and his only alternative is to
take the place of the strikers. The
unions thereupon reach an agreement
—are called to work, and our men are
turned away. All the Afro-American
asks is that he shall be allowed a
chance; that he shall be taken on his
merits, and we shall have no vagrants
and few criminals among us. He does
not ask for donations, but he pleads
an equal chance with the rest of his
white countrymen to earn a dollar.
Somebody at the head of great firms is
now and then driving the unfortunate
Negro to desperate conditions. Some
body, animated with the old spirit of
slavery and caste, joins the enemy in
waging a cruel war for the degrada
tion and starvation of a poor and de
fenseless race struggling to better
conditions. But the individual who
conspires against a brother man earn
ing bis own living is answerable for a
monstrous, moral offense against God
and man. President Patterson, of the
National Cash register Co., of Dayton.
0., must some day answer.

CONGRESSIONAL.
The Philippine tariff debate in the
house on the 11th consisted more
of party maneuvering for advantage
ous campaign material than of discus
sion of the question at issue. The
tariff was the text of a speech
by Mr. Grosvenor and of an extended
reply by Mr. Williams, the minority
leader. Before going into executive
session the senate listened to a speech
by Mr. Hyburn in support of his bill
creating a national board for the con
trol of corporations.
Interest was injected into the
Philippine tariff debate in the house
of representatives on the 12th by a
Massachusetts “idea” tariff .exposi
tion by Mr. McCall, of that state; by
a character study of the Filipino by
Mr. Longworth, of Ohio, and a de
fense of President Roosevelt by Mr.
Pou, a democrat from North Carolina.
Besides these there were a number of
speeches delivered on the merits of
the bill, nearly all of which were in
opposition to the measure.
There were nine speeches in the
house on the 13th on the Philippine
tariff bill, consuming nearly six and
one-half hours. Three of the speakers
opposed the bill and six favored it.
General debate on the Philippine
tariff bill was concluded in the house
on the 15th, having been in progress
daily since January 4. The statehood
fight made its appearance on the
floor for the first time in the form of
a personal explanation by Mr. Bab
cock, of Wisconsin, leader of the
opponents of joint statehood.
The Philippine tariff bill was
passed by the house on the 16th, sub
stantially as it came from the ways
and means committee. The vote was
278 to 71. Rice was made subject to
the same tariff as sugar and tobacco —
25 per cent, of the Dingley rates. Un
expectedly the senate found itself
considering the railroad rate question,
which was precipitated by Mr. Fulton
taking the door to make a brief speech
in explanation of an amendment of
fered by him to the Dolliver bill.

Awful Tragedy in a New
Hampshire Home.
FAMILY MURDERED
Charles Ayer, a Farmer, Killed Seven
Persons and Then Com
mitted Suicide.
Pembroke, N. H., Jan. IS.—Seven
persons, all members of the family of
Charles Ayer, are supposed to have
perished in a fire which yesterday de
stroyed Ayer's farmhouse, near here.
The bodies of a child and of Ayer's
mother-in-law have been found in the
ruins. Mrs. Ayer and four children
are missing and it is feared that they,
too. are victims of the fire.
The theory of the county authorities
Is that Ayer was the murderer, but up
to a late hour they had been unable to
find any evidence to indicate the
methods employed to wipe out the
family. Whether the victims were
shot, or killed by other means, cannot
be told at present. Up to a late hour
only charred fragments of two of the
victims had been recovered, although
persons who visited the scene of the
fire thought that they observed two
other trunks in the blazing ruins. The
victims of the tragedy were:
Charles F. Ayer, aged 43, killed
himself by shooting.
Mrs. Addie Ayer, his w’ife.
Mrs. Isaac Lakeman. Ayer’s mother
in-law.
Flossie Ayer, aged 12.
Alfred Ayer, aged 10.
Bernice Ayer, aged 6.
Andrew' Ayer, aged 4. and a girl
baby, all children of the Ayers.
The fire occurred about 9 o’clock in
the morning and Ayer drove up to the
home of his sister. Mrs. George Bailey,
In the town of Chichester, about six
miles from his home, just after 10
o'clock. He remained at MrH Bailey’s
place during the afternoon and when
informed that his buildings had been
burned manifested some agitation. A
moment later he draw a revolver and,
pointing it at bis right temple, fired
and fol! unconscious. He died last
night.

ON THE FIRST BALLOT.
Clement Fallieres Is Elected President
of France.
Paris, Jan. 18. —The national assem
bly met yesterday in the palace at
Versailles for the election of a presi
dent of the republic. The assembly
consists nominally of 591 deputies and
300 senators, but owing to deaths, ill
ness and the passage of some of the
deputies to the senate, leaving their
seats vacant, the number present was
decrease of < about 850, making 425
votes necessary to elect a new presi
dent.
M. Clement Armand Fallieres, pres
ident of the senate, was elected, re
ceiving 449 votes to 371 for M. Dou
mer, president of the chamber of
deputies.
In all 849 voters were present. The
final figures were: M. Fallieres 449,
M. Doumer 371. Scattered 28. One
voter abstained from depositing his
ballot.
M. Fallieres returned to Paris from
Versailles escorted by a military
guard of honor. He will take over his
new duties February 18. He is G 4
years of age and is noted as an orator.
