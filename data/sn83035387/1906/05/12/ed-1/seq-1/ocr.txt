BEAT GIRLS
Over the Head, and
Kicked Them, Too.
The “Controller” for
Our Men’s Heads.
Experiences on the “Jim Crow"
Street Cars in “Dear Old
Georgia,” at Atlanta.
Atlanta, Ga. —A few nights ago a
local street car conductor and motor
man beat two Afro-Americans over
the head, kicked them from the car
and then shot one of them. It seems
that they had been talking too loud to
suit the ideas of the conductor. Time
was in the south when our people
dreaded the Ku-Klux, and rightly so,
because of the horrors perpetrated by
that “chivalrous” band of cutthroats,
but now it is the visage of another
class that inspires fear in their hearts.
There is no more irritating cause of
friction today between the races in
the south than that produced by the
generally infamous conduct of street
car conductors and motormen toward
our people. Practically every city has
its “Jim Crow” seats, and we who are
forced to ride at times take these
seats because the law says we must.
We are obeying the law and trying to
gel to our destination as quickly as
possible. Once in that car we are
at the absolute mercy of the conduc
tor, who treats us just about as he
pleases. If two are conversing even
in an ordinary tone we are liable to be
approached in a surly and bullying
way and ordered to “keep quiet.” If
3 white passenger comes in, and all of
the seats for whites are taken, the
conductor does not hesitate to order
some Afro-American passenger to get
up. If by reason of his own careless
ness the Afro-American is carried a
block or two beyond his desired stop
ping place and the least thing is said
about it, there is going to be trouble
for that passenger. Any one of these
three things may result in th? bring
ing into play of the “controller.” Now,
the "controller” is the thing. It was
intended to be used by the motorman
in controlling the car, but it figures
almost as frequently as a means of
beating us over the head. If people
like the couple above spoken of who
were engaging in. conversation do not
stop instantly when ordered to do so
the conductor yells at us that he is
going to throw us off the car. If we
say one word in reply to this threat
he rushes at us as if about to strike
us and most likely takes hold of us
or one of us for the purpose of putting
his threat into execution. The passen
ger most naturally resists, ar d then
comes in the controller, for the man
at the front of the car with that in
strument- in his hands has been
watching what was going on behind
him. He knows what is expected of
him, and he commences to rain blows
on the passenger’s head: and not only
on the head of that particular one but
on those of any other Afro-American
who might be in arm’s reach. When
the steps are reached the passenger,
bruised and sometimes bleeding, is
shoved off into the street, while the
car bobs on. This very thing hap
pened to two young Afro-American
girls on a car in this city about a
month ago. They were on their way
to work, and the cause of their being
beaten and kicked, so. they were
actually kicked, was simply because
'they were laughing and talking with
each other. It so happened that they
worked for white people who, when
,they heard of the occurence, took
steps to prosecute the conductor and
motorman. But there are dozens of
cases in which no action whatever is
taken. The law gives the conductor
and motorman police power, and they
use it for all it is worth and seem to
gloat over the fact. The regular
policemen, be it said to their credit,
exercise fairness in their treatment of
Afro-Americans, and only on seldom
occasions are heard of as using their
clubs, but the street car conductor de
lights in the use of the deadly iron
crank. —Thomas H. Malone in N. Y.
Age.

COL. ALLEN ALLENSWORTH,
A Veteran of the Civil War—Chap
lain of the 24th Infantry—Re
cently Retired as Lieutenant
Colonel.
Rev. Allen Allensworth after serv
ing 20 years as chaplain of the 24th
Infantry, U. S. army, was recently re
tired from service. He was appointed
by President Cleveland, his applica
tion having been on file during Presi
dent Harrison’s administration. Chap
lain Allensworth was promoted to
major June 14th. 1904. He served in
the U. S. navy from April 3, 1863, to
April 4, 1865. The retiring clause of
the congress act, approved April 23,
1904, gave him on retirement, April
7, this year, the grade of lieutenant
colonel. He was the only chaplain in
the army with a civil war record and
has attained the highest rank ever
held by one of the race in the regu
lar service (army). When appointed
chaplain, Rev. Allensworth was pas
tor of Union Baptist church, Cincin
nati. With his family, he has located
at Los Angeles, California. Rev.
Washington E. Gladden, an Afro-
American Baptist minister of Den
ver, Col., succeeded him as chaplain
of the 24th infantry.

teemed contemporary, the Louisville
(Ky.) American Baptist, for the ex

at the head of this article.

A Fine Musician.
Chicago, Ill.—The four year term
class in composition at the Chicago
musical college numbers 62. Prof. N.
Clark Smith is one of the b^st six
composers in the class whose com
positions furnished the program for a
recent entertainment. Two of his ef
forts were sung by a member of the
college faculty. This is the first time
in the history of the institution that
an Afro-American's music has been
presented and accepted. Mr. Smith's
graduating certificate was marked
100, the best average obtainable.
Three of his new plantation songs
have been accepted by one of the
largest music publishing houses in the
city. He is the leader and director
of our finest band and orchestra.
