A BROADQUESTION
Golden Opportunity to Get the
Best Homes—Ought to be
Grasped.
While talking with a business man
(white) a short time ago, our conver
sation gradually turned to the Afro-
American and his future. “Why is it.”
he asked rather bluntly, “your people
always seek employment where they
are always kept in a strained condi
tion? I mean barbers, porters, wait
ers and such, and strained because of
the necessary tidyness usually in
volved in such employment. Why
haven't you any farmers such as we
have?” I listened meditatively, and
he continued: “Don’t you know that
some of our most noted, most inde
pendent and important factors in
building up our nation are men who
(in a practical way) give us our daily
maintenance?” “But,” I managed to
venture while squirming out of a dif
ficult position, “a farmer is never
thought much of by his city brothers.
His clothes are always dirty and the
barnyard odor is sickening, and —and
etc.” It was a very lame effort I was
making, as I knew he was speaking
the truth, and one finds it a hard task
to combat truth successfully. “The
greatest pleasure I have,” he resumed
without noticing my feeble argument,
“is a drive out to my farm once or
twice a week and see how my man is
getting on and ” Here he was in
terrupted by a customer coming in
and which gave me a chance to with
draw, which I did with much gratifica
tion, as there is nothing more distaste
ful to me than discussing the
shortcomings of our race with a w'hite
man. But the conversation had served
its purpose. Those “whys” cer
tainly looked puzzling to me, and, I
soon found myself asking the same
question. I have read all the papers
published for and by our race, and 1
cannot recall seeing a market report
on product, hogs, cattle, sheep or any
sort of grains. Nor have the facts
been recorded that Mr. Jacob Jingler
and Ham Hustler shipped one or two
carloads of hogs or sheep or cattle to
the Chicago markets recently. Or has
It been stated that the colored farm
ers are busy these days taking stock,
wheat, hay or sugar beets to market.
Again, nothing said about the recent
purchase of a quarter section adjoin
ing the farm of Rufus Richman by
Sam Sparecash, who intends stocking
it with a fine herd of cattle or a flock
of sheep. Or, we forgot to state in
our last issue, that Mr. Ephram Plenty
brought us in a nice sample of po
tatoes grown on his farm, and said
(wreathed in smiles) that he expected
to harvest between 300 and 500 bush
els per acre, and he had planted five
acres. And that Mr. Isaac Doowell
left us a fair sample of his turnips
and onions (one bushel each) and cab
bages, which in our mind were the
largest we had ever seen. Isn't that
inspiring? Now, all you readers who
have noticed any such sentences in
our race papers, please hold up your
left hand. Contrary, stare vacantly
before you. Would not our race pa
pers assume a more interesting aspect
were such mentions made? Wouldn't
we jealously scan the columns in
search of the most progressive pro
ducer? Wouldn’t we welcome and dis
cuss weekly news more cheerfully and
feel that we were before the world as
a nation equal to the most progres
sive, instead of reading the now fren
zied paragraphs which leave one in a
discouraged mood? Wouldn’t our dear
editor read with much gratification
each bit of news sent in, and instead
of the four-page edition be forced to is
sue eight pages, even though he had
to charge more for it? Just ask him.
It seems almost impossible nowadays
to read a race paper without laying it
aside with a heavy heart and a feeling
that we are the most despised nation
on earth. Yet our editors can do no
better, as the race is doing nothing to
furnish them with better subjects. It
is a conceded fact that the only race
difficulties we have in the north are in
the overcrowded cities where both
classes are struggling for the sami
dollar, and as the whites hold the best
positions, they in many instances
reach out and get the dollar while the
poor colored man is getting ready. Go
into our small communities where
there are only a handful of our people
and there is no race problem; even
where intermarriage comes into con
sideration. It is the man, what he is,
and what he intends to do, that gov
erns. Our colored farmers have the
utmost regard paid them by their
brother farmers and merchants, and
his products are eagerly sought, and
he receives the highest market price.
Comparing the city man with the
farmer, -we will suppose that there are
two merchants, barber or such, one
white, the other black, in a white com
munity. Who will get the most trade,
even though the black man has the
finest stock? Who will retire in wealth
first? Again, place these same two
on adjoining farms with the colored
man having the best stock or grain.
Along comes a buyer. He sees those
nice fat hogs, sheep or cattle on one
side and those long, gaunt animals on
the other side. Which place will he
turn into first? A white financier told
me recently that he would gladly loan
our colored farmer any money he
wanted without even his note. But
this man (colored) doesn't need it.
Since my article in The Gazette two
years ago, calling attention to our
cheap western land, I have succeeded
in inducing many of otjr people to
come out here and they are well on
their way to independence. Land sell
ing then at $5 and $3 is now $7 and
sl2 per acre, and is still rising in
price. Feeling the need of our hard
working people now crowded in the
cities and held there for lack of
knowledge of where they could better
themselves, and do it very cheap, 1
have secured 25,000 acres of as fine
land as can be found anywhere, same
to be sold in tracts of 20 acres and up
ward, and have placed an advertise
ment elsewhere in this paper. I have
an unlimited amount of reading mat
ter you may have just for the asking.
I want to reach the laboring man, the
dependent woman, tup renter who is
ih’nedd of a home and who enjoys out
door life. Begin with 20 acres and in
crease as you can. We must have
more farmers and producers. There
is no other way of showing your true
worth only through contributing to
the welfare of the nation. Secure
some of our real substantial national
independence—land. Let me send you
some of my folders and you can see
how easy it is to get a start.
FRANK E. DIXON,
Faribault, Minn.
CARROLL TO TILLMAN.
An Open Letter to t‘ e “Blatherskite”
Senator from One of His Con
stituents.
Columbia, S. C. —R *v. Richard Car
roll, of this city, recently issued the
following open leter to Senator Ben
Tillman of this state:
“You must remember, Senator,
when you became a candidate for gov
ernor of South Carolina, many of the
Negroes voted for you, and others
staid away from the polls rather than
cast their ballots against you. If the
Negroes had rallied to the polls that
year, under the leadership of the
white men, whom ’bey had among
them, you would never have been gov
ernor of this state or senator of the
United States; but tiwy looked upon
you as their friend, jf you will recall
your speeches during ftmt memorable
campaign, you will s >e that no white
man has ever come on the political
platform in South Carolina and made
such a defence for the Negro as you
made. I myself was one of those men
who urged every Negro that 1 came in
to contact with not to vote against
you. But you haw# turned against us.
I would to God this night that you
would reconsider, and that you would
speak a kind word, wherever an op
portunity comes to you to do so in
defense of that rac^, that has been
tittle to the southern xyhite people as
well as the United States government.
You do not need us as a subject for
a lecture. There is no race problem
that cannot be solved. Men like you
can solve this problem by urging the
people of this country to give the Ne
gro justice. We do not ask for social
equality or any special favors (though
we should have special favor), but jus
tice and every encouragement should
be given by you and others to the
self-respecting Negroes in the south,
who are law-abiding and who are
trying to make something of them
selves. You know, again, Senator,
that there is no danger of Negro su
premacy in the south, or anywhere
else in this country, when the major
ity of the people are white, and when
the majority of the people have the
property and the money and the in
telligence and everything else to
make them secure. You know very
well that there is no danger of the
Negro getting ahead of the white man,
when the white race has had over a
thousand years of freedom and we
have had only 40 years. To beat us
backward, to retard our progress, and
to rouse prejudice against us will in
jure the white race as much as or
even more, than it will us. The white
people have more to lose than we
have. Your race should help our
race. We are all related as God
created of one blood all the people on
the face of the earth. We are human,
and intensely human, and that is why
we have so many criminals among us.
We are kind to humanity. You must
also remember that there are good
citizens, who are contributing to the
welfare of the commonwealth; in oth
er words, are paying taxes, and are
Christians.”

SENATt IN A ROW.
Senators Foraker and Lodge in Tilt
Over President’s Report on
Brownsville Affair.
Washington, D. C., Dec. 19. —The
president's special report on his re
cent dismissal from the army of the
three companies of troops of the 25th
infantry was the storm center of de
bate in the senate Wednesday after
noon. When the reading had been
concluded, Senator Foraker asked that
the message and documents be re
ferred to the committee on military
affairs with instructions to take such
testimony as was deemed necessary
to establish the accuracy of all the
facts. In a moment the senate was in
excitment. Senator Lodge objected
to the form of the motion. He said if
a congressional investigation was to
be ordered he would invoke the rules
and have the matter go over. Finally
the Foraker motion was divided. The
message and papers were sent to the
committee on military affairs. The
senator from Ohio then sent up his
resolution for an investigation. Sena
tor Carmack opposed leaving the ques
tion to the discretion of the commit
fore ordering an inquiry that might be
long and expensive. Senator Foraker
said he would press consideration of
the resolution Thursday.
Senator Tillman’s Talk.
Senator Tillman delivered his paid
lecture, ‘‘The Race Problem,” at
Grays’ armory Monday night before
an audience that filled one-third of the
house. The lecture was a repetition
of his recent Chicago address. There
was no disturbance.—Cleveland Press.
