THE FLOWER GARDEN.
The Permanent Flowering Plants Re
quire the Least Care and in the
End Cost the Least.
May is the great month in the flower
garden, for most flower seeds are planted
during the first two weeks in May. The
exceptions are those extremely hardy
things like sweet peas, mignonette,
Drummond phlox, sweet alyssum and
California poppy, which may even be
sowed the fall before for earliest spring
blooifi.
To many people a flower garden
means only a small inclosed place where
chickens and dogs are not supposed to
break in and where backache and annual
flowers are cultivated. Yet the whois
lot upon which the house stands is, or
ought to be, a garden, and the relative
importance of the various departments
of an outdoor living room are about as
follows:
1. The trees and shrubs near the
house and on the borders of the place.
2. The vines on the walls and porch.
3. The lawn, free from flower beds.
4. The informal border of hardy per
ennials.
5. The annual flowers that fill in th*?
gaps.
6. The coleus and other gaudy bedding
plants.
The nlost permanent plants, like the
flowering trees, shrubs and vines, are the
ones that require the least care, and cost
the least in the long run, as compared
with annual flowers, the seeds of which
must be sown every year. It is human
nature to think last of trees, shrubs and
vines, because their first, cost is greater,
but let us resolve to put all our spare
money and strength into the permanent
things this year and depend on the
bounty of friends, if necessary, for
flower seeds. —Wilhelm Miller.
To Take Out Iron Rust.
To take out iron rust dip the spot
into a strong solution of tartaric acid
and expose to the sun. When dry
wet the article with warm soapsuds;
rub the stain with ripe tomato juice,
expose to the sun again, and when
the stain is nearly dry wash in more
suds. This is a good method.

PAUL LAURENCE DUNBAR.
The Poet, Author and Reader Suc
cumbs to Stomach Trouble and
Consumption—Sick Seven
Years.
After an illness of seven years, the
last three of which he suffered great
ly, Paul Laurence Dunbar, of Dayton,
the poet, died at his home there at
3:30 p. m. last week Friday. Con
sumption and stomach trouble were
the causes of death. Despite his ill
ness, Mr. Dunbar kept at his work in
termittingly and wrote his last poem
just before Christmas for his book
entitled, “Howdy, Honey, Howdy.”
His best known poem is, “When Ma
linda Sings,” which was dedicated to
his mother.
His Best-Known Works.
His authorship includes the follow
ing: “Oak and Ivy,” published in 1593;
“Majors and Minors,” 1895; “Lyrics of
of the Lowly Life,” 1896; “Folks
From Dixie," 1898; “The Uncalled,”
1898; “Lyrics of the Hearthside,” 1899;
“Poems of Cabin and Field,” 1899;
“The Strength of Gideon,” 1900; “The
Love of Landry,” 1900; “The Sport of
the Gods,” (novel) 1901; “The Fana
tics,” (novel) 1901; “Candle-Lightin'
Time,” (poems) 1902; “Lyrics of Love
and Laughter,” (poems) 1903; “Heart
of Happy Hollow,” 1904; “Li'l Gal,”
(poems) 1904; “Lyrics of Sunshine
and Shadow,” 1905; “Howdy, Honey,
Howdy,” 1905.
Mr. Dunbar was born in Dayton
June 27, 1872, and made money
enough to attend school by selling
newspapers and running an elevator
in a hotel. Although he began to
write verse when seven years old his
work did not attract attention until
he was in the high school. His first
effort of note was “Oak and Ivy.”
Dayton High School Graduate.
He was graduated from the high
school, Dayton, in 1891 and was mar
ried to Alice Ruth Moore, in New York,
March G, 1898. He worked for news
papers and later gave public readings
of his poems. Besides his wife, he
leaves his mother and two half broth
ers, Robert and William Murphy, of
Chicago.

With a Bullet—Poor Soul Rushed In
to Eternity—“ Playing with Fire”
—Fearful!
Memphis, Tenn.—Coming from the
rural districts of Arkansas an Afro-
American sauntered up Front street
through this city recently. He had
not often visited the “Bluff” city and
was unaccustomed to the views which
attracted his gaze. The great govern
ment and banking houses were things
of wonder and astonishment to him.
His eyes peered in wildest curiosity
into the show windows, where re
volving urns and dazzling images be
wilder the hard-fisted toiler from his
plantation home. The Afro-Ameri
can could not realize the meaning of
so much opulence and splendor, but
intently he gazed, little thinking that
anything other than joy and pleasure
lurked along his pathway as hp
meandered through the thoroughfares
of this metropolis. Enchanting
visions lured him on. Delirious with
delight it never once entered his
dreams that as a sight-seer and in a
place where pleasure seemed to be
supreme, he might encounter a des
perate foe. He happened to be stand
ing in the way of a vengeful white
man who jostled him from the side
walk. The Afro-American shouted
out; “That ain’t the wav we do where
I came from. We pass around each
other.” Enraged at the seeming “im
pudence” of the plantation man. the
white man hurried on and reported
the case to a patrolman, Peter Mc-
Keon, adding that a Negro was selling
stolen jewelry on Front street. Suit
ing the case exactly to a heartless and
depraved nature, the patrolman told
the white man to “go ahead and grab
the Negro” and that he would “come
up and arrest him." The patrolman
handed him his pistol telling him to
hurry on. They both reached Front
street near the Illinois Central depot.
As the white man approached the
Afro-American the latter fled. The
former fired upon his poor fleeing
victim. He followed up the chase,
leaving the patrolman behind. An
other volley rang out as the Afro-
American ran into the Choctaw Com
press. He staggered into the willows
at the foot of Washington street and
died. The shot passed into the back
of the Afro-American and through his
heart. The unknown white man dis
appeared. Nothing is done and no one
knows anything of this fiendish mur
derer. Such deeds are every day in
ducing crime and indiscriminate
slaughter among the people. • It is
gradually turning the south into a
i field of slaughter and desolation. It
is playing with fire which must yet
I break out into conflagration despite

Americans boast.
