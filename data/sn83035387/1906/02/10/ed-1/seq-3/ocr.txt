Max S. Hayes’ Sensible Talk.
Henry T. Eubanks’ effort, one even
ing last week to “square himself” with
the local labor unions caused a “peck
of trouble” for at least one member of
the organization and that one was
President Mclntyre, of the Trades and
Labor Council. It seems that Eu
banks had asked to address the body
in his own defense. A protest against,
granting his request from Electrical
Workers union No. 38 was read just
before his arrival. This organization
held that Eubanks was not friendly to
organized labor. Pres. Mclptyre was
its delegate to the Trades council and
although he delivered an address, pro
testing bitterly against permitting Eu
banks to speak, handed in his resig
nation, declared the meeting adjourn
ed and left the hall, the Trades and
Labor council remained in session to
hear Eubanks’ talk. Mclntyre seemed
alone in his action. The strong point
of Eubanks’ address was the state
ment that some unions drew a color
line, and that that was the reason
why more of our men were not mem
bers of trades unions. In reply to this
statement Delegate Max S. Hayes
said:
“While in some unions the color line
is drawn, the American Federation of
Labor prohibits the drawing of the
color line. The line is not generally
drawn by the printers. There was
never a time when we tried to prevent
Negroes from being union men.
Thousands of mine workers are col
ored men. The same is true of the
building laborers and other crafts in
the building industry. In this central
body we have never drawn the color
line. We have had colored men here
as delegates ever since the trades
council was formed.
“When a union takes action to keep
out men because of their color it tends
to injure that union. It is absolutely
immaterial whether their faces are
black or white. I believe we are go
ing to continue in that line regardless
of petty jealousies.”
Hurrah! for Max S. Hayes. “That’s
the talk.” It’s ring is correct and right.
In the course of his address Mr.
Hayes stated that he didn't know of
any circulars that had been sent down
to Columbus against Eubanks to de
feat him for the position of assistant
sergeant-at-arms.
”1 protest against such ward heel
ing methods,” he said. “I always have
been against them and always will. 1
am sure the circulars were not sent by
the United Trades and Labor council."
Fred B. Hobby of the Waiters’ union
and Business Agent Lloyd of the bar
bers’ union also stated that their un
ions drew no color line and gave in
stan < s to prove their contention. We
hope Messrs. Hobby and Lloyd are

Phipps’ $10,000,000 Gift.
New York City.—Nearly the entire
block in the “Sa<i Juan” district on
the West Side is to be used for the
construction of the second and third
huge tenement houses provided for in
the $10,000,000 gift made by Henry
Phipps. Plans for the structures, to
be occupied exclusively by our people,
were filed some weeks ago. Each
building will accommodate 173 fami
lies and will contain 510 rooms, larger
and more sanitary than the most
stringent laws demand. The low
figure of $1.35 a month for each room
will be charged. The apartments con
tain from two to four rooms each.
Private baths are planned for 60 per
cent, of the apartments. Gas ranges
will be in all of them; steam heat,
store rooms, shower baths, spacious
laundries, kindergartens, play grounds,
four roof gardens, private halls a
foot wider than required by law,
windows eight to ten inches in
excess of legal demands, and a host of
other innovations are provided.

McGoverns’ Manager “Talks Sense.”
Chicago, Ill.—“I tell you, there is
nothing in drawing the color line.
Who were the three greatest fighters
at their weight in this country at one
time but the three colored men, Geo.
Dixon, Joe Gans and Joe Walcott?
“We don’t care what Nelson will do.
If we win on March 14, we will give
Joe Gans a chance; but he must weigh
in at 133 pounds ringside. Why should
Terry give this man at least 11 or 12
pounds the best of it? As it is, Gans
will have the best of the weight by 7
pounds when he consents to weigh in
at 133 pounds ringside. As long as
McGovern is in my charge, he will not
be forced to give too much away.”
Thus spoke Terry McGoverns’ sensible
manager a few days ago.
