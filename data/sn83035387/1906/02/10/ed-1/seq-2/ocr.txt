CRAZY TOM DIXON.
This man Dixon we have recently
learned was not an apt and bright stu
dent while at school, but a boy inclin
ed much to caricature and exaggera
tion. As an essayist he sought to be
sensational, and as a delineator he
placed the ideal for the real, ofttimes
substituting falsehood for truth. He
was an alarmist as well as an extrem
ist and not infrequently terrorizing
the minds of his fellow students with
some frightful foreboding of the im
possible. He delighted to have around
him groups of listeners, who regarded
him as being visionary and unsafe as
a leader. From any point of view he
was pessimistic and a purely eccentric
being. Were his relations with the
people the sr me as those sustained
with his fellows at school, he would
be better understood and known as a
mere mediocre and a man of small
calibre, either Intellectually or mor
ally. If he has ever advanced a single
great thought in all his mad thrusts
against the American Negro, the
world has not yet discovered It. He
Indulges now in the same idle flings
against our people that he was wont
to do, when at school he made the
abuse of the Negro boy his hobby. He
has finally become a confirmed Inebri
ate, so extremely Intemperate upon
the race question that he never finds
time to deal with any matter save that
of hounding the Afro-American from
pillar to post. He hears the call of
the hunter’s horn and at a single
bound he is on the trail of his victim,
yelping and barking with no certainty
as to whether he had scented a harm
less lamb or the carcass of some
worthless traducer. Tom Dixon has
not lost his reason, he has not pos
sessed It to that degree to enable him
to adduce an original thought. He de
pends rather upon those attainments
which he acquired from long disci
pline and by singleness of purpose. He
has attained distinction as a maligner
and a trailer, yet like a mad demon
and a monomaniac, breeding discord
and venting his spleen against his
brother man. Oh, says he, “if I were
keeper of hell I would not admit a Ne
gro into its confines.” This poor worm
of the dust offers such a declaration
as a home thrust to exclude the Ne
gro from any place in God’s universe.
The remark is not only the emanation
of brutality, but it is the outcropping
of a depraved and Insane mind. To
aay the least it was cowardly, because
it would never have been uttered
through fear of a dreaded enemy, but
through a morbid and truckling sensi
bility toward the “superior” race, to
whom all sycophants very naturally
bow. No one can truthfully deny that
Tom Dixon is a dlssensionist and a
consummate traitor to humanity. He
has no honest conception of his ob
ligation to mankind, for like a devour
ing beast, he ventures forth to destroy
his brother in black only to gratify
the propensity developed in his
boyhood days. Author of "The
Leopard Spots,” he offers now his
latest work, “The Clansman,” with
plans similar in purpose and nature to
that of the old Ku Klux Klan, which
proved itself so destructive and venge
ful under the leadership of Gen. Bed
ford Forrest, of Memphis, Tenn., the
chief of Negro haters. Dixon endorses
the cruel and bloody methods of the
Ku Klux Klan in “The Clansman” and
recommends their reorganization.
But the better element of the south
protest against such madness.
when certain citizens of Birmingham,
Ala., informed him that an organiza
tion of the “Sons of Clansman” has
- been formed, he sees the perfidy of his
crime; he pauses and then writes to
his misguided allies whom he has so
shamefully beguiled and says: “I fear
the organization of the ‘Sons of Clans
man' is a little premature.” This bad
man shows the design of an evil heart,
especially so since he plots desolation
and death against his own fellow
countrymen. But he pauses at the
magnitude of his terrible crime and
pleads that he fears that the under
taking is "premature.” Premature!
Premature, to begin the butchery of
southern negroes. With bewitching
Insinuation he coils himself in the
work of fiction and like a serpent
fastens his fangs in the bleeding, pros
trate form of the much wronged and
^persecuted Negro, and demands that
,hc shall now die at the hands of the
midnight assassin. But conscience
wbjpa the man like a guilty miscreant.
rußy realizing that the sentiment of
the country is not in sympathy with
Jxis treasonable designs, he bewails
the mingling ot white and colored peo
together in northern cities and
oondoie* the action of four southern
in Georgia who, be
says, are now favoring amalgamation
of the races. He feigns to be alarmed
for American civilization and in pitl
ous whining declares that at the in- :
stance of Bishop Gailor, of Tennessee, !
he has been importuned to lend his in- I
fluence to save southern civilization, '
from the corruption of the Negro.
What imbecility! What blindness in
the man, that the growing wealth, the
broad intelligence, the culture and
high civilization of a grand and pow
erful race are to deteriorate and be
come corrupted by a people, the crea
tures of one blood and of God’s own
creation. But Dixon offers no protest
against the clandestine meeting of
southern white men with southern
colored women. He sees no shame in
the evil of such unlawful cohabitation.
He finds no time to impose restraints
upon ihose who violate laws. Without
one plea for justice or the mutual
maintainance of the moral law and
the brotherhood of man, he wages a
relentless fight against those to whom
he is called to rescue and to save.
Blind to every sense of duty and
honor as a citizen and a man, and hav
ing no concern as to the interior
workings of the nation and the mighty
evils of religious caste, of idolatry
and Godlessness, he beats the wind
and spreads the deadly leprosy of so
cial disease, discord, disaster and
desolation among the people.

BLAIR'S SUCCESS.
His “Pierre” Hogs the Best—The Fin
est on Sioux City Market—En
couraging.
Fairbanks, S. D. —The following is
from the Sioux Oily Journal of Satur
day :
“Interest at the stock yards yester
day was centered in a load of hogs re
ceived from 31 miles north of Pierre,
S. D. They were shipped by B. P.
Blair, a progressive (Afro-American)
farmer of Hughes county. A more,
flattering compliment could not be
paid to this apparently new industry
of that section than the fact that these
hogs commanded the top of the market.
This was the second load of hogs to
be shipped out of Pierre, the other
load being sent out by Mrs. P. F. Mc-
Clure last September. This consign
ment of hogs plainly indicates that the
j corn belt is constantly moving west
and north. It was a surprise to those
in the trade that such excellent qual
ity and finish as were displayed in this
load of hogs could come from this sec
। tion.
“These were Poland China hogs and
crossed the scales at a weight of 300
pounds per head and sold at $5.22%
per 100 pounds, netting Mr. Blair in
the neighborhood of sl6 each. John J.
Murphy, who bought them for the
Cudahy Packing Co., ventured to say
that they were the best hogs received
for the day, and that he was confident
they would dress out a large per cent.,
as they were well fattened and as hard
as it is possible to make a porker.
“Mr. Blair was pleased that he
landed at the top of the market. He
said that during the capital campaign,
when there was a great deal of mud
slinging, it was asserted that nothing
could be raised in the vicinity of
Pierre. He wished that those who
thought this true could have been at
his farm this fall during harvesting
time. He had in 50 acres of corn, on
which these hogs were fattened, that
went 45 to 60 bushels to the acre; he
had 100 acres of wheat from which he
threshed 2,200 bushels; his oats aver
aged SO to 90 bushels to the acre, and
he had 30 acres of speltz that panned
out 40 bushels to the acre. Besides
raising this grain, he said that he grew
a large amount of all kinds of garden
truck. Mr. Blair said that a number of
his neighbors were raising hogs and
meeting with great success. He also
runs a brand of cattle. What he turn
ed off last fall he sold at home, but he
had a shipment of range cattle during
the 1904 season on the Sioux City
market.”
