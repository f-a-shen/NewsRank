The above is an excellent portrait
of Hon. Robt. P. Kennedy, of Belle
fontaine, who is a candidate for the
republican nomination for secretary
of state. “King Bob” became famous
as lieutenant governor in 1886, when
by his bravery and boldness he held
the democrats in check in the senate
when a personal encounter was mo
mentarily expected. Before the end
of his term as lieutenant governor he
resigned to accept the nomination for
congress in his district, and served
two terms in the house. The general
was elected lieutenant governor when
Judge J. B. Foraker, now Ohio's senior
United States senator, was a candi
date for the second time for governor,
being defeated the first time largely
as a result of stories circulated against
him by democrats. Soon after his
second nomination. Judge Foraker
wrote the editor of The Gazette to
call on him in Cincinnati, his home,
for a conference anent some of these
stories. On our arrival there, the day
appointed for the meeting, we learned
from his brother that Gen. Ken
nedy had come down from Bellefon
taine and prevailed upon the judge to
accompany him home at once, where
the citizens of Bellefontaine, that
county and vicinity, had arranged a
large reception and public meeting for
them. At the judge’s request, we
went from Cincinnati to Bellefontaine,
and here, at the close of the meeting,
occurred an incident (about 20 years
ago) which is worth while relating
at this time. When the editor of The
Gazette arrived in Bellefontaine, he
registered at the leading hotel, where
Judge Foraker and other prominent
republicans were stopping. If mem
ory serves us correctly, the proprietor
was a Kentucky democrat by the
name of MiltenUerger. After dinner,
among those we met in the lobby of

Bellefontaine to report for his paper
the meeting and particularly the
speech of the republican candidate
for governor. Judge Foraker. Before
going to the meeting the editor of
The Gazette attempted to secure a
room in the hotel, but was told that
all were taken. Mr. Boyle, who hap
pened to be standing near, very
promptly told the clerk that the editor
was there at the special request of
Judge Foraker, and that if no other
room was available, his room should
be placed at his disposal, and that he
could sleep with him. He thereupon
offered his room key. Meantime the
editor had decided to return to
Cleveland on a midnight train, and
thanking Mr. Boyle refused his kind
offer. At his urgent request, the edi
tor accompanied him to the meeting
and occupied a seat at the press table
in the grand stand which h»d been

The incident developed out of the
attack which Tillman made on the
enforcement of law in Illinois, and
more particularly in Chicago, in the
Walsh bank case. Hopkins defended
the courts and administration of jus
tice in his state, and followed it up
with a denunciation of Tillman for
publicly upholding the murder of Af
ro-Americans in his state whose only
crime v as their effort to exercise their
constitutional prerogative of voting.
Quite apart from the merits of the
subject matter under discussion, there
is intense feeling of satisfaction over
the manner of Hopkins’ attack on
Tillman, and the evident result of
Anally and fully beating Tillman into
a corner, where he was ready to ad
mit, by manner and tone, that he was
whipped.
For days Tillman has launched forth
into sensational discourses and at
tacks on different individuals and
states. The senate tired of It long
ago, but it must be admitted that Till
man had, in effect, bulldozed the sen
ate by his sharp tongue and brutal
methods- of speech-making attack.
The situation needed just such a per
sonality, and just such a man as Sen
ator Hopkins to meet it. He fought
Tillman with his own weapons—bitter
denunciation, open and expressed
contempt in tone and manner and ex
plicit and repeated charges of unfair
ness and demagogy, which Tillman
could not avoid.
More power to Hopkins!
