The speech of ex-Speaker J. Warren
Keifer, of Springfield, in congress on
March 15th favoring the reduction of
the congress representation of those
states (southern) guilty of disfran
chisement, was exceptionally exhaus
tive and strong. Mr. Keifer is a re
publican of the “old school.” Would
that there were more of his kind In
congress. There would then be some
probability of legislation compelling
the practically rebellious states re
ferred to, to obey the mandates of the
United States constitution bearing
upon the suffrage of citizens.
The action of Congressman James
R. Mann., of Chicago, who recently led
the fight that defeated the bill in the
house of representatives to pay the
heirs of Samuel Lee who was elected
to congress in 1880 but denied admis
sion until the closing hours of the 47th
congress, the SIO,OOO salary due him

has refused to pay, ought to move our

MISSOURI’S SHAME.
We sincerely trust that Gov. Folk,
of Missouri, is sincere in his declara
tion to hang at least the leaders of
that moti of 3,000 men and boys who
lynched those three Afro-Americans
at Springfield, Mo., recently. Two of
the victims who were charged with
assault upon a white girl are now be
lieved by the police and others of that
city to be innocent. The woman could
not Identify them as being the brutes
who attacked her. Even if they were,
they were not as bad as the barbar
ians who led the mob, battered down
the jail doors, broke windows, ham
mered off locks, committed other dep
redations, hung the three victims
from a light tower In the public square
which was surmounted by a sheet Iron
figure of the Goddess of Liberty and
finally burned the bodies of the vic
tims, all three of whom were innocent
of committing any crime or even mis
demeanor. This is terrible! Then its
treatment of the sheriff’s wife, re
ferred to elsewhere in this paper!
That was also outrageous, barbaric!
And a strip of a boy, a mere lad, 18
years of age, a veritable young devil,
led the mob! We trust that the gov
ernor’s activity and determination to
vindicate the law, and his reward of
S3OO for the arrest and conviction of
each participant in the fiendish and
hellish orgy will produce results, the
kind that the decent, law abiding citi
zen of the entire country will welcome
and praise him for, for all time.
THE RIGHT TO THE BALLOT.
Under the above heading the Chi
cago Chronicle published an editorial
on April 9 which is worthy of repro
duction in thfe editorial columns of
The Gazette. There is so very much
of the kind of truth in it that should
have the widest circulation just at
this time, especially among our states
men and leading thinkers, that we
' would that it were in our power to
make them all read it carefully and
thoughtfully. It is as follows:
“The distinguished white men who
delivered addresses last week at the
celebration of the twenty-fifth anni-
versary of the founding of the Tuske
gee institute said some wise things
and gave the Negroes plenty of good
though trite advice along with some
that was not altogether judicious .
. They all told the young Negroes
whom they were addressing that they
must first become educated and edu
cated chiefly up to economic inde
pendence—that they must make them
selves indispensable as workers in the
fields and shops and become good,
humble, toiling citizens, suppressing
higher aspirations, before they would
become fairly entitled to the bajlot.
And they gave scant encouragement
to look for anything political beyond
the ballot, though they assured the
Negroes that they were in the coun
try to stay . . This teaching .
. calmly assumes that the ballot is
something to be given only to those
who know how and are disposed to use
It wisely for the benefit of less en
lightened and moral people and is not
given to everybody because he needs it
himself. The fact is that men want
and need the ballot for their protec
tion against wrong and oppression.
The right to a voice in choosing those
who make and administer the laws—
which i« what 1* meant by the “bal
jot’’ in this discussion—was demanded
before this republic existed, first by a
select and powerful class for its own
better protection, then by a larger and
less select class for the same pur
pose, and so on to universal manhood
suffrage. As a means of defense it
was needed as much —nay, more—by
the lowest and most oppressed class as
by the highest which had been divest
ed of political rights. This is histori
.cally true irrespective of our own po
litical experience. It was in recogni
tion of this truth that, the right to vote
was guaranteed by the fourteenth and
fifteenth amendments to our national
constitution. The right to vote was
given to the Negro not because he was
educated or wise, for he was neither,
but because he needed it for his pro
tection against those who denied him
civil rights and the protection of the
laws and sought to reduce him to a
slavery more cruel and barbarous than
that which the thirteenth amendment
sought to abolish forever.”

The Gazette in the South.
Elizabeth City, N. C.—Editor Gazette,
Dear Sir: Several months ago my
husband, Capt. H. A. W. Mebane, sub
scribed to your paper for me. I have
been intending to write you how very
much I am pleased with it and es
pecially your manly and courageous
editorials. I think a clean-cut, newsy
paper, backed up with intelligence and
courage, standing for and defending
the rights and principles of humanity
is not only an honor to the editor but
a blessing to the race. I am sure our
people, only a few years up from slav
ery, are proud of such men as your
self and will stand by them; especial
ly the better class. Accept, please, my
humble but sincere congratulations,
and though way down in the old
North state we have men and women
that think as you do and stand for
what you stand, the highest develop
ment and equal rights of the Negro.
Sincerely,
L. V. MEBANE.

“MUCK RAKE” ADDRESS.
The widely heralded and sensational
“muck-rake” speech of President
Roosevelt was delivered at the laying
of the corner stone of the office build
ing for the house of representatives
at Washington. Recognizing the ex
istence of evils, social and economic,
the president urges warfare, but de
plores the actions of those who make
indiscriminate attacks on men of
business or public life, declaring such
a course would only react in favor
of the unscrupulous. The huge for
tunes being amassed in this country
are regarded as a menace to public
welfare and a progressive tax is
urged whereby these fortunes should
be curtailed and limited to a certain
amount. Rich men are also warned
of the danger in attempting to pre
vent the regulation and control of
their business in the interest of the
public.
