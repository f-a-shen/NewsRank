LINCOLN INSTITUTE NOTES.
At no previous time in the world's
history has that ancient and honor
able occupation known as agriculture
received the scientific attention that
has been accorded it within the last
decade; and yet, after all has been
said, although it is universally ad
mitted that the farmer is the only
real master of his destiny, farming
methods at present, in spite of ad
vanced ideas, are, as someone has
well Said, little more than “ A slight
scratching of the soil.” To render
this “scratching” more complete,
more productive of results, is the aim
of the farmers’ institutes and conven
tions that have multiplied within the
last few years. By means of the ideas
thereby disseminated the white farm
er has been enabled to make two
grains grow where only one grew be
foi’b; and this should indicate what
the Negro farmer can do if he will
learn to be wise in his day and gen
eration; if he will learn proper cul
tivation and fertilization of soils, ro
tation of crops, etc. Hence the an
nual call for a farmers’ convention,
issued by President Allen, and to be
held at Lincoln Institute Friday, No
vember 9, 1906, should meet with a
hearty response from all interested ip
the future of the Negro.
Every school house, as has been
demonstrated again and again by
Missouri’s energetic and able state su
perintendent of public instruction,
Hon. W. T. Carrington, should join the
new crusade for better methods in
agriculture, the oldest of arts and
sciences, the corner stone of all civ
ilization. To this end let us come
together as men and women and dis
cuss ways and means of acquiring
farming lands, of improving the con
ditions of farm and home life, etc.
Remember the date, Friday, Novem
ber 9, 1906, Lincoln Institute.
