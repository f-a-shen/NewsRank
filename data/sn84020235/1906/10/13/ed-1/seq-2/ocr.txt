VIRGINIAS WERE FRIGID
Snow and Sleet in New York, While
Four Below Freezing Was Scored
at Elkins, W. Va.—Cotton
Crop Damaged.
Washington—A cold wave swept
over the east and south Thursday, and
broke all records for this season in
many sections. In many sections the
severe cold snap was accompanied by
snowfall, and a snow and sleet that
raged in upper New York state, par
ticularly, caused great damage. Down
along the Blue Ridge mountains, in
the two Virginias, was the coldest of
the country, and at Mount Weather,
the mercury was down to freezing,
while four below freezing was scored
at Elkins, W. Va. North of Lake Su
perior was another cold region.
Storm Brought Death.
Buffalo —Joseph Kerbei, a bachelor,
was killed and an unknown man was
shocked to death by coming in contact
with live wires, which had been brok
en by the w’et snow.
Snow General in Canada.
London, Ont.—High winds cut a
swath 20 feet wide through this city,
damaging houses. No fatalities. Heavy
snow general.
Blizzard in Pennsylvania.
Pittsburg—Twenty-eight degrees and
a blizzard, with six inches of snow,
was the record in portions of Penn
sylvania Thursday.
Made Cotton Jump.
New York —New high records were
reached in the cotton market again,
and trading was active. The sales
were about 500,000 bales, according to
estimates, and the market closed from
on advance of 27 to 40 points, with
all months selling above the 11-cent
mark.
The impression undoubtedly exists
in local circles that temperatures have
been low enough to prevent any fur
ther development of the plant over an
important area in the south.
Serious Damage in Oklahoma.
Guthrie, Okla. —Advices from impor
tant cotton sections of Oklahoma and
Indian territory are to the effect that
the crop was materialyl damaged by
this week’s frost. The crop was prac
tically made, but much of the cotton
has not yet matured, and the plant in
that stage will be killed. A conserva
tive estimate gleaned from the reports
places the yield at one-half bale per
acre.
Georgia Lost Thousands of Bales.
Atlanta, Ga. —President Harvie Jor
dan, of the Southern Cotton associa
tion, estimates that between 40,000 and
50,000 bales of cotton were killed in
Georgia alone by Wednesday night’s
cold snap.
Cotton Damaged by Frost.
Memphis, Tenn. —Cotton has been
damaged in northern Mississippi, Ar
kansas and Tennessee by frost.
BOOKER T. WASHINGTON’S VOICE,
The Negro Race Must Get Rid of the
Criminal and Loafing Element.
New York —Boker T. Washington
said before the Afro-American associa
tion:
“In the season of disturbance and
excitement, if others yield to the
temptation of losing control of their
judgment, and give way to passion
and prejudice, let us, as a race, teach
the world that we have learned the
great lesson of calmness and self-con
trol.
“I repeat what I have uttered on a
recent occasion, every iota of influ
ence that we possess should be used
to get rid of the criminal and loafing
element of our people, and to make de
cent, law-abiding citizens.”
Wreck of a Fast Mail.
St. Louis —In a wild run at a speed
of 60 miles an hour to make up lost
time, the Iron Mountain fast mail
leaped from the track at the Caronde
let curve at 3:16 a. m. The engineer,
John Casper, who 17 minutes before
had begun his first run on the fast
mail, was killed. Peter Rafferty, the
fireman, just out of the hospital after
injuries in a similar wreck last Febru
ary, was seriously scalded. Nine mail
clerks were injured. The train was
behind time and was endeavoring to
make it up.
To Be Flagship of Pacific Squadron.
San Francisco —Heralding her ap
proach a thousand miles away, by
means of wireless telegraphy, the pro
tected cruiser Charleston came into
port. The Charleston comes here to
be the flagship of the Pacific squadron.
Three Men Killed in Explosion.
New York—Three men were killed
and a dozen others were rendered un
conscious by an explosion and fire in
the Pennsylvania railroad tunnel un
der Long Island City. The injured
were rescued with difficulty.

A COLD-BLOODED MURDER.
Young Teacher Killed in the Presence
of Her Pupils.
Cleveland, O. —In the presence of 60
pupils, in the South Euclid school,
narry Smith, 25 years old, shot to
death Miss Mary Shepard, a teacher,
22 years of age. Cornered behind the
barn in the rear of his home in
Warrensville, two hours later, Smith
shot himself through the head, dying
instantly.
Four Cleveland policemen, with
drawn revolvers and unuder cover of
eight armed farmers, were about to
rush on young Smith when he ended
his life.
Disappointment in love is said to
have been the motive for the cold
blooded murder.
